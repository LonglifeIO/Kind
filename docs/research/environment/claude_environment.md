# Environment Design for Io at Probe 1
## A research document for Kind, framing candidate designs across the eight open questions

This document addresses environment design for Io's first probe, written so the choices made now do not foreclose Probes 2–4. The substrate is settled (continuous-Gaussian RSSM with GRU deterministic state, posterior/prior split, free-bits ELBO, latent-disagreement actor over a small ensemble, no scalar reward, no continuation predictor, decoder retained, fixed-length episodes) and the harness is settled (Mind-on-Mac with mirror reading everything, Io's actor reading only concat(h_t, z_t), four telemetry streams with world_event walled off). The job is to specify the environment that fits these decisions and that lets the testable claims of later probes — that builder-perturbations come to register with a different statistical signature than internal stochasticity, that offline/dream processing yields useful structure, that something resembling intentional engagement can be observed — be made meaningfully.

A note on translation. Most of the literature relevant here was written inside a reward-driven frame: agents survive, accumulate return, are evaluated against benchmarks. Where a finding survives translation out of that frame, it is reported below. Where it does not — where the published claim is inseparable from reward shaping or capability metrics — that is named rather than smuggled.

## Q1 — Observation modality

**(a) What the question is really asking.** Given that the decoder is kept (so the mirror can decode dream rollouts into something humans can read), and given Kind's interest in engagement-from-somewhere as a substantive commitment rather than a metaphor, what observation channel will let a small RSSM build non-trivial latent structure while keeping the decoder's job tractable?

**(b) Concrete options from the literature.** Three families are well attested. *Pixel-based small images* are PlaNet's and Dreamer's native input (Hafner et al., 2019; Hafner et al., 2020; Hafner et al., 2023). DreamerV3's standard configuration uses 64×64 RGB observations and a CNN encoder, but DreamerV3 also runs unchanged on proprioceptive (low-dimensional vector) inputs with an MLP encoder, and the same hyperparameters work in both regimes — which is the empirical evidence that the RSSM family is not pixel-dependent. *Low-dimensional factored state vectors* are the observation form for proprioceptive DM Control tasks and are also the BabyAI/MiniGrid default, where each grid cell is encoded as a 3-integer tuple (object, color, state) rather than as pixels (Chevalier-Boisvert et al., 2018). *Partial-perspective sensors* — a fixed-size egocentric window that does not cover the whole environment — are the MiniGrid default (a 7×7 forward-facing window) and are used in BabyAI, in the Animal-AI testbed, and in egocentric Dreamer variants used in maze tasks (Beyret et al., 2019; Crosby et al., 2020; Van de Maele et al., 2023).

The phenomenological tradition is clear that perception is from somewhere — Merleau-Ponty's *Phenomenology of Perception* makes embodied perspective constitutive of perception rather than an add-on (Merleau-Ponty, 1945). Pfeifer and Bongard (2007) operationalise this in robotics: morphology and sensor placement are not implementation details but partially determine what categories the agent can form. Enactivism (Thompson, 2007; Di Paolo & Thompson, 2014) extends this: the agent's perspective on the world is constitutive of sense-making. None of this tradition specifies a pixel grid size; what it specifies is that *some* perspective be drawn, that there be a foreground and a horizon.

**(c) Tradeoffs against Kind's commitments.** Pixels keep the decoder honest in a way humans can inspect — the mirror can show frames of dream rollouts, which is precisely what Probe 3 will need. But pixels with a small RSSM and free-bits regularisation can encourage the model to spend latent capacity on visually salient but structurally trivial features (Deng et al., 2022, on DreamerPro, documents this for DM Control with distractor backgrounds). Factored state vectors give the cleanest learning signal but make the decoder's output non-legible-as-image, which weakens the mirror's role at Probes 3–4. Partial-perspective sensors satisfy the phenomenological commitment most directly and also keep the observation small (7×7×K with small K is cheap to encode and decode), at the cost of forcing the RSSM to do partial-observability inference from the start.

**(d) Suggestion / unresolved.** A defensible choice is *partial-perspective sensors rendered into pixels*: a 7×7 to 9×9 egocentric window, rendered as a small RGB image (e.g. 32×32 or 48×48 with 4–8 pixels per cell). This honours the phenomenological commitment (Io sees from where it is, not God's-eye), keeps the decoder image-based so the mirror can decode dream rollouts to legible frames, and is well within RSSM precedent (MiniGrid's RGBImgPartialObsWrapper does exactly this; PlaNet/Dreamer routinely handle 64×64). It does mean Probe 1 already exercises partial observability — but that is a feature, not a bug, because the RSSM is built for it (PlaNet's design rationale is precisely that the latent state should disambiguate partial observation).

## Q2 — Action space

**(a) What the question is really asking.** Given a small RSSM and a latent-disagreement actor with no preference prior, how rich must the action repertoire be for the latent to come to represent something other than action statistics, and for "engagement" to mean anything?

**(b) Concrete options from the literature.** Discrete action spaces in Dreamer-family agents typically range from 4 (cardinal moves) to ~17 (Crafter: 4 movement + 13 interactions; Hafner, 2021) to 18 (Atari). MiniGrid uses 7. DM Control uses continuous action vectors of dimensionality 1–21. DreamerV3 uses identical hyperparameters across discrete and continuous settings (Hafner et al., 2025). On the embodied-cognition side, Pfeifer and Bongard's design principle of "ecological balance" claims that the complexity of the body (including action repertoire) must roughly match the complexity of the morphology and the environment — but they offer this as a heuristic, not as a numeric lower bound. Enactivism's notion of structural coupling (Di Paolo & Thompson, 2014) says only that there must be enough action variety for a *coupling* to form — that is, for the agent's actions to make a difference to subsequent observations in a way the agent can come to track. Empowerment (Klyubin, Polani & Nehaniv, 2005) gives a formal handle: the channel capacity from actions to future sensors must be non-trivial. With one-bit actions and no environmental contingency, empowerment is zero.

**(c) Tradeoffs.** Too small an action space (e.g. 2 actions) will make the RSSM's latent collapse onto observation statistics with action treated as nuisance. Too large (e.g. 20+ discrete actions in a 5×5 grid) gives an action space larger than the meaningful state space, which encourages the actor to look like a noise generator and the disagreement signal to be dominated by under-visited action stubs rather than world-model uncertainty. The "smallest action repertoire that supports intentional engagement" is not given a numeric value anywhere in the embodied/enactive literature; the literature treats the question qualitatively.

**(d) Suggestion / unresolved.** A discrete action space of 4–6 is the defensible band: 4 cardinal moves; optionally a "wait/no-op" and one "interact" if Probe 2+ wants resource manipulation. This sits inside MiniGrid/Crafter precedent and inside Dreamer's well-tested discrete-action regime. Below 3 actions the latent has too little for the dynamics predictor to model; above ~8 the disagreement signal dilutes in a small environment. The lower bound is empirical, not derived; the literature does not give a principled floor.

## Q3 — Pressure without self-continuation reward

**(a) What the question is really asking.** Without reward, without death, with fixed-length episodes — what makes the world non-trivial for Io to engage with? What pressure produces interesting trajectories without smuggling reward back in as a "drive"?

**(b) Concrete options from the literature.** The active-inference literature distinguishes pragmatic value (preference-driven) from epistemic value (uncertainty-reducing); with a uniform preference prior, only epistemic value remains, which is exactly Kind's setup (Friston et al., 2015; Da Costa et al., 2020). Plan2Explore operationalises the epistemic part as latent-disagreement variance over an ensemble (Sekar et al., 2020) — already the actor's signal. Pathak et al. (2017, 2019) and Schmidhuber's earlier work give curiosity-as-prediction-error variants; these are vulnerable to the noisy-TV problem (Burda et al., 2018; Mavor-Parker et al., 2022), which matters here because Q4 explicitly asks for environmental stochasticity. Empowerment (Klyubin et al., 2005; Salge et al., 2014) gives a non-curiosity intrinsic frame: keep one's options open, maintain influence — formally action-future mutual information. Novelty search (Lehman & Stanley, 2011) and quality-diversity (Cully & Demiris, 2018) operate at the population level rather than the single-agent level, so they translate weakly to a single core agent; their relevance is conceptual (divergence over convergence). Oudeyer's intrinsic-motivation programme (Oudeyer, Kaplan & Hafner, 2007; Baranes & Oudeyer, 2013) recommends learning-progress signals that drive a developmental robot toward regions of moderate predictability; this is closer to what an ensemble-disagreement signal will produce than to a noisy-TV-vulnerable prediction-error signal. The artificial-life tradition (Tierra: Ray, 1992; Avida: Ofria & Wilke, 2004) shows that *ecological structure* (resources, interaction, spatial geometry) can produce open-ended pressure without an explicit reward function — but in those systems the pressure is selection over populations, which Kind has ruled out.

**(c) Tradeoffs.** Pure novelty / disagreement on a small static world will saturate quickly: once Io has visited every cell and done every action a few times, disagreement collapses and the actor degenerates. Empowerment-style signals are robust to this but expensive to compute and would amount to adding a second intrinsic objective beyond ensemble disagreement, which the substrate has settled against. Resource scarcity ("things to gather, things that disappear") gives the world internal dynamics that keep latent disagreement non-trivial without becoming reward — provided no consumption signal feeds back into the actor as bonus. Slow drift in environmental parameters keeps the world model from saturating without imposing scarcity.

**(d) Suggestion / unresolved.** Make the world *contingent* rather than *rewarding*: place a small number of resource items that regrow stochastically, allow Io to interact with them but emit no scalar bonus, and let the world's state-distribution shift slowly across an episode (regrowth rates, item locations). This is the developmental-robotics move (Oudeyer, Kaplan & Hafner, 2007) translated out of reward-shaping: the world is interesting because there is something to predict, not because predicting it pays. Kind should accept that without scarcity-or-drift, ensemble disagreement on a 5×5 grid will collapse within a few hundred steps and the actor will look uniform. That is not a reward smuggled in — it is the world having internal dynamics.

## Q4 — Internal stochasticity

**(a) What the question is really asking.** What kind and how much randomness must the environment carry so that (i) Probe 4's distinguishability claim is testable in principle and (ii) the world-model does not learn the world as deterministic and then have nothing to be uncertain about, while not being so noisy that the model learns nothing?

**(b) Concrete options from the literature.** The Dreamer family handles stochastic dynamics by design: the prior z_t is a distribution and the posterior reads observations to refine it. DreamerV3 explicitly addresses high-stochasticity environments via KL balance and free bits (Hafner et al., 2025). The noisy-TV literature (Burda et al., 2018; Mavor-Parker et al., 2022; Pathak et al., 2019) documents what kinds of stochasticity break curiosity-style intrinsic motivation: *action-coupled* unpredictable stimuli (the agent can produce noise on demand) trap prediction-error agents indefinitely. Latent-disagreement (Plan2Explore) is more robust than prediction-error to this, because aleatoric (irreducible) noise produces *agreement* in the limit — every ensemble member learns the same noise distribution — whereas epistemic uncertainty produces *disagreement*. Sancaktar, Piater and Martius and others document this distinction explicitly (Sancaktar et al., 2022). Stickier action environments (Machado et al., 2018) introduce stochasticity that is independent of the agent's choices — useful but limited. Drift-in-parameters (slow non-stationarity) is well-treated in the developmental-robotics literature as a way to keep learning-progress signals alive without making the world unpredictable per se.

**(c) Tradeoffs.** Per-step pixel noise is the worst choice — it inflates the decoder's reconstruction loss without giving the dynamics anything to learn, and on a small RSSM with free bits it encourages posterior to track noise rather than structure. Coarser stochasticity — events with low per-step probability and clear semantic units (a resource appears, an item moves a cell) — is what Dreamer-family models handle cleanly because the stochastic latent z_t can carry a few bits of state about whether such an event occurred. The line between "stochastic enough" and "washes out structure" is not given a number anywhere in the literature; what is given is the qualitative recipe: keep stochasticity *event-like* and *low-rate* relative to the agent's step rate, and keep it independent of action so the noisy-TV trap is avoided.

**(d) Suggestion / unresolved.** Two stochastic processes, both event-like: (i) resource regrowth at a low Poisson-ish rate (e.g. expected 1–3 events per 200-step episode), independent of Io's actions; (ii) optional weather-like global flag (a single bit toggled with low probability per step, affecting some observable property of all cells uniformly — colour tint, say). Both are easy for the RSSM to model in z_t and both give the world a non-trivial signature that Probe 4's perturbation-detection claim can be made against. The exact rates are not derivable from literature; they should be tuned so that ensemble disagreement does not saturate within an episode and so that posterior KL does not get clamped at the free-bits floor.

## Q5 — Builder-perturbation surface

**(a) What the question is really asking.** Probe 4 wants Io to come to model the statistical signature of builder-as-perturbation as distinct from internal stochasticity, with no marker in the observation space. So the difference must live in the *statistics*. What hooks should the harness expose, and what statistical signature should builder events carry?

**(b) Concrete options from the literature.** The closest precedents are caregiver-as-scaffold in developmental robotics (Ugur et al., 2015; Nagai & Rohlfing, 2009; Tanaka et al.), where a human modifies the environment to support the robot's learning. These are interaction studies, not detection studies — they do not ask the robot to *distinguish* caregiver perturbations from natural dynamics. Sancaktar et al. (2024) on regularity-detection comes closest in spirit. The interactive-learning literature (Knox & Stone; Christiano et al.) treats human input as an explicit reward signal, which Kind has ruled out. The active-inference / generative-model literature on "agency detection" (e.g. self-other discrimination via prediction signatures: Friston et al., 2018) gives a relevant theoretical handle: an agent with a generative model of the world can in principle separate perturbations whose statistics do not fit any internal cause from perturbations that do, but only if the two have distinguishable signatures. There is no canonical published recipe for "perturbations that an RSSM can come to distinguish from internal noise without an observation marker." This is a Kind-original research question; the literature provides ingredients, not a precedent.

For the statistical signature itself, the available levers are: *distribution shape* (e.g. internal noise is Poisson-event-like; builder events are heavy-tailed in magnitude or spatially clustered), *temporal correlation* (internal noise is i.i.d. or weakly autocorrelated; builder events come in bursts or at non-uniform inter-event intervals — a Hawkes-like process), *magnitude tail* (internal events affect one cell; builder events can affect a region), and *type-of-change* (internal: resource regrowth; builder: object insertion / object removal / state alteration — a different kind of state change in the world's transition kernel).

**(c) Tradeoffs.** If builder perturbations are *too similar* to internal stochasticity, Probe 4 has nothing to distinguish in principle. If they are too different — say, types of state change that *only* the builder ever makes — then Probe 4's distinguishability is trivial (the agent isn't learning a statistical signature, it is reading a category label from the dynamics). The sweet spot is partial overlap: builder events use the same vocabulary of state changes as internal stochasticity (resource appears/disappears, object moves) but their joint distribution over (when, where, what) differs. This is testable: train the world-model on long no-perturbation rollouts, then ask whether it assigns lower likelihood to perturbed segments than to non-perturbed segments.

**(d) Suggestion / unresolved.** The harness should expose four mutators as a minimum: `add_resource(cell)`, `remove_object(cell, type)`, `set_cell_state(cell, state)`, and `introduce_novel_object(cell, type)`. Each mutator emits a structured event into world_event with timestamp, type, location, and the pre/post local state. Frequency: Probe 1 should be perturbation-free or near-zero so the world's natural-stochastic signature is established cleanly; Probes 2–4 ramp perturbation rate. The signature differences should be specified now (so the harness shape is set): builder events should be *temporally clustered* (bursts of 2–4 within a short window, with long quiet periods) where internal stochasticity is uniform, and should occasionally use the `introduce_novel_object` mutator (which has zero baseline rate from internal stochasticity — this is the "tail" of the signature). The unresolved question is whether the substrate's small RSSM can in fact pick up such a signature; that is what Probe 4 tests.

## Q6 — "Small enough" for Probe 1

**(a) What the question is really asking.** The Probes document mentions a 5×5 grid with one resource type. Is that big enough to give the RSSM something to model, or so small that it either trivially solves the world (boring dream rollouts) or has too low a dynamic-range to learn anything interesting?

**(b) Concrete options from the literature.** Published RSSM-style world models target environments roughly two orders of magnitude larger. PlaNet's smallest task is DM Control's cartpole-balance with a continuous proprioceptive state of dimension ~5 and a continuous 1-D action — but the *visual* version has 64×64 RGB pixels. Dreamer's Atari tasks are 64×64×3. Crafter is 64×64 cells with a 7×9 partial view, observed in pixels (Hafner, 2021). MiniGrid environments range from 5×5 to 19×19. The smallest environment in the published RSSM literature with non-trivial latent dynamics is, to our knowledge, MiniGrid-Empty-5x5 — and even there, RSSM-class agents are not the standard baseline; the standard baseline is PPO with a small CNN. There is no published study that asks "what is the smallest grid for which a continuous-Gaussian RSSM with free bits learns a non-trivial latent." The closest empirical anchor is Hafner et al. (2025): DreamerV3's smallest published task uses model size "S" (~8M parameters) and runs on standard benchmarks. The paper does not characterise a lower bound on environment size below which the model collapses.

The artificial-life literature (Avida, Tierra) and ALife-classics on minimal environments suggest that very small environments saturate evolutionary novelty quickly (Standish, 2003). This is suggestive — small worlds *do* run out of structure — but the dynamics are different from RSSM learning.

**(c) Tradeoffs.** A 5×5 grid with one resource type has roughly 25 cells × small number of resource configurations × ~4 agent positions × 4 orientations — order of a few thousand discrete states. With a continuous-Gaussian latent of dimension 30 and a deterministic h of dimension 200 (typical small-Dreamer settings), the latent has more capacity than the state has bits. Two failure modes are real: posterior copies prior (KL clamped at free bits, latent carries no information) is easier when the world has too few bits to encode; alternatively, the model overfits and dream rollouts are brittle. Going to 8×8 or 10×10 with 2 resource types substantially raises the world's bit-content without making the environment large in any meaningful sense.

**(d) Suggestion / unresolved.** The literature does not settle this. A defensible Probe 1 default is an 8×8 or 10×10 grid with 2 resource types (or 1 resource type plus a movable object), 200–500-step episodes, partial 7×7 view. This gives the RSSM enough state to model without saturating in a few episodes, and remains "small" in the sense Kind intends — a single human can watch every cell, every step, and form a clear expectation of what should happen. The Probes document's 5×5 with one resource is probably below the threshold; this is judgment, not derivation, and Kind should be prepared to revisit if Probe 1 telemetry shows latent collapse.

## Q7 — Temporal structure

**(a) What the question is really asking.** Probe 3 will couple dream-to-wake ratio to the builder's actual life rather than to a hardcoded environmental rhythm. Probe 1 should not foreclose that coupling. What minimal temporal structure can the environment carry now without baking in a rhythm Probe 3 will need to override?

**(b) Concrete options from the literature.** Crafter has a day-night cycle (Hafner, 2021); Animal-AI has none by default; MiniGrid is timeless. The biological literature on circadian and ultradian rhythms (Aschoff, Czeisler, Pittendrigh) shows that organisms exhibit endogenous rhythms even in constant environments, and that environmental rhythms entrain rather than create them. Computational work on temporal structure in RNNs/RSSMs (e.g. multi-scale RSSM variants: Gumbsch et al., 2024) shows that hierarchical world-models benefit from environments with multiple timescales of variation — but this is benefit-for-task-performance, which is outside Kind's frame. There is no literature recommendation we found for "the minimal temporal structure that doesn't preclude later coupling to an external clock"; the question is novel in this form.

**(c) Tradeoffs.** Hardcoding a day-night cycle now creates a periodicity Io's RSSM will absorb into z_t and that Probe 3's dream-to-wake coupling will then have to either overlap with or override. No temporal structure at all gives an a-temporal world where the only time-marker is Io's own action history — clean for Probe 1, but Probe 3's coupling will introduce a *new* kind of temporality the model has never seen. Slow drift in a single global parameter (the "weather bit" of Q4) gives a non-periodic temporal marker that Probe 3's coupling can either align with or vary against, and that does not commit to a rhythm.

**(d) Suggestion / unresolved.** Keep Probe 1 a-temporal in the strong sense: no day-night cycle, no fixed period. Allow only the slow, aperiodic drift of Q4's weather-bit-or-equivalent. This leaves the design space for Probe 3's coupling fully open. If Probe 3 wants to introduce a builder-coupled rhythm later, it can do so without conflicting with an environmental clock the agent has already learned. The literature does not recommend this; nor does it recommend against it. It is a design judgement consistent with Kind's "capacity-over-exercise" commitment.

## Q8 — Minimum environmental complexity for the specific RSSM

**(a) What the question is really asking.** Below what threshold does this specific RSSM (continuous-Gaussian z, free bits, small encoder, kept decoder, ensemble disagreement signal) either collapse — posterior copies prior, KL pinned at free bits — or overfit to the point that dream rollouts are trivially deterministic?

**(b) Concrete options from the literature.** PlaNet is reported to work on DM Control suite tasks with image inputs; the smallest of these has continuous proprioceptive states of dimension ~5 and 1-D continuous actions, observed as 64×64 pixels (Hafner et al., 2019). Dreamer (V1/V2/V3) has been trained on Atari, DM Control, Crafter, Minecraft, Memory Maze, BSuite (Hafner et al., 2020; Hafner et al., 2021; Hafner et al., 2025). DreamerV3's robustness work explicitly cites free-bits-plus-KL-balance as the mechanism that prevents posterior collapse, but does not characterise an environment-side lower bound below which collapse occurs anyway. Reconstruction-free variants (TD-MPC: Hansen et al., 2022; TD-MPC2: Hansen et al., 2023; DreamerPro: Deng et al., 2022) make different tradeoffs that are out of Kind's substrate scope but their ablations on DM Control with distractors confirm that *too much irrelevant variability* hurts representation quality — which is the upper end of the same phenomenon.

The continuous-Gaussian latent has known weaker multimodal handling than the categorical latent (DreamerV2/V3) — averages "left" and "right" into a "middle" hypothesis (the multimodal-averaging problem; Hafner et al., 2021 motivates the V2 switch on these grounds). In a small, low-multimodality environment this matters less; in a deterministically branching one it matters more. Free bits set at ~1 nat per latent (per Dreamer convention) means roughly 1 nat × latent dimension of "free" KL — for a 30-dim latent, ~30 nats of headroom. If the world's per-step bit-content is below this, KL will sit at the free-bits floor and z_t will carry less information than h_t carries — which is the collapse regime.

**(c) Tradeoffs.** The 5×5 single-resource environment from the Probes document has very low per-step bit-content (a handful of bits at most). That is not a fatal verdict — the world-model can still learn — but it sits close to the regime where free-bits dominates and the stochastic latent does little work. Going to 8×8 with 2 resource types and the Q4 stochasticity (event-like regrowth, weather bit) takes the per-step bit-content to ~10–20 bits and gives z_t something to do. Going larger (Crafter-scale, ~64×64 cells) would put the model squarely in published-precedent territory but conflicts with "small before scale."

**(d) Suggestion / unresolved.** Two coupled choices are defensible. *Conservative*: 8×8 grid, 2 resource types or 1 resource + 1 movable object, event-like stochasticity, partial 7×7 view, latent dimension reduced from Dreamer-default (e.g. stochastic latent dim 16 instead of 32) so free-bits headroom stays comparable to world bit-content. *Closer to literature*: 10×10 to 12×12 grid, full Dreamer-default latent dimensions, partial view. Below 8×8 with single resource and no stochasticity the model very likely collapses to trivially deterministic dreams; above 16×16 the design starts to violate Kind's smallness commitment. The threshold is not derivable; literature gives the regime, not the number.

## Synthesis — three candidate environment designs

Each candidate is a coherent set of choices across Q1–Q8. They are not strictly ordered; they trade off legibility for the mirror against richness for the model against fidelity to ethological / phenomenological framings.

### Candidate A — "Tabletop"
A small a-temporal contingent world, optimised for Probe 1 cleanliness.

- **Q1**: Pixel-rendered partial-perspective egocentric view, 7×7 cells rendered to 32×32 RGB. (Phenomenologically grounded; mirror-decodable.)
- **Q2**: 4 discrete actions: forward, turn-left, turn-right, interact.
- **Q3**: 1 resource type that regrows stochastically; no scarcity-based drive.
- **Q4**: Event-like regrowth (Poisson, low rate, action-independent); no other noise.
- **Q5**: Builder hooks as specified in Q5(d); Probe 1 runs perturbation-free.
- **Q6**: 8×8 grid, 200-step episodes.
- **Q7**: A-temporal. No periodicity; no weather flag.
- **Q8**: Stochastic latent dim 16, deterministic h dim 200, free bits ~1 nat per latent dim.

*Tradeoff profile.* Maximises legibility for human inspection of dream rollouts. Minimises confound: there is exactly one source of stochasticity, so Probe 4's signature-detection claim has the cleanest possible baseline. Risks under-exercising the RSSM — disagreement may saturate within an episode if regrowth is the only thing happening — and forecloses some richness Probe 2 may want. Most "small before scale" of the three.

### Candidate B — "Weather-grid"
A small a-temporal contingent world with two stochastic processes, optimised for Probe 4 distinguishability.

- **Q1**: Same as A.
- **Q2**: 5 discrete actions: 4 cardinal moves + interact (no separate turn — orientation is the move direction).
- **Q3**: 1 resource type regrowing stochastically + 1 movable object Io can push.
- **Q4**: Two stochastic processes — event-like resource regrowth and a global weather bit toggling at low rate (visible as a uniform tint in the rendered observation). Both action-independent.
- **Q5**: Same hooks as A. Builder events designed to use the same vocabulary (regrowth, object move, novel-object insertion) but with bursty temporal correlation distinct from the resource process and with occasional novel-object insertion that has zero baseline rate.
- **Q6**: 10×10 grid, 300-step episodes.
- **Q7**: A-temporal in the periodicity sense; the weather bit gives aperiodic slow drift but no fixed cycle.
- **Q8**: Stochastic latent dim 24, deterministic h dim 256, free bits per Dreamer convention.

*Tradeoff profile.* The weather bit is non-trivial structure for the RSSM to learn (a single bit of slow latent state), and gives Probe 4 a richer baseline of "internal stochasticity" to compare builder perturbations against. Two stochastic processes raise the bar for Probe 4: Io must come to model both internal sources before a perturbation signature can register as "neither of these." Slightly less legible than A — the mirror's dream rollouts now show weather changes that may look surprising if a human doesn't know the rule.

### Candidate C — "Ethological mini-arena"
A small world structured after the Animal-AI / ethology tradition rather than the gridworld tradition.

- **Q1**: Pixel-rendered first-person 2.5D arena, 32×32 to 48×48 RGB, with a small number (3–5) of distinct object kinds Io can perceive and approach.
- **Q2**: 4–5 discrete actions: forward, back, turn-left, turn-right, interact.
- **Q3**: Objects of different kinds afford different interactions (one regrows, one moves on contact, one is static); no reward; affordances yield observable consequences only.
- **Q4**: Event-like state changes per object kind; one slow-drift parameter (e.g. ambient lighting) varying aperiodically.
- **Q5**: Same hooks; mutators target specific objects.
- **Q6**: A 6×6-meter arena with 3–5 object kinds (analogous to a one-room Animal-AI scene).
- **Q7**: A-temporal in periodicity; lighting drift is the only temporal signal.
- **Q8**: Stochastic latent dim 24, deterministic h dim 256.

*Tradeoff profile.* Most faithful to the ethological / phenomenological framing — Io has an arena, not a grid; objects are objects, not cell-types. Most aligned with how comparative-cognition tasks have been constructed (Beyret et al., 2019; Crosby et al., 2020). Costs: more engineering surface (a small physics or pseudo-physics simulator); the decoder has more pixels of detail to reconstruct, which can pull free-bits headroom into surface details rather than dynamics; furthest from "small before scale" of the three, though still small by the RSSM literature's standards.

### Tradeoffs between the candidates

*Legibility for the mirror.* A > B > C. Candidate A's dream rollouts will be the easiest for a human to inspect cell-by-cell. C's rollouts will look like small video clips; B sits between.

*Richness for the RSSM.* C > B > A. Candidate C gives the RSSM the most heterogeneous structure to model; A gives it the least.

*Cleanliness for Probe 4 distinguishability.* A > B > C, but only because Probe 4 is *easier to set up* in A. Whether Probe 4 can succeed at all is more plausible in B and C, because there is more internal-stochasticity structure for the model to come to know before a perturbation registers as foreign. This is the most important tension to surface: the cleanest setup for Probe 4 may also be the one where Probe 4's positive result is least informative, because there is little internal structure to be distinguished from.

*Foreclosure of later probes.* C forecloses the least — it has affordance variety, slow drift, and arena geometry that Probes 2–4 can exercise. A forecloses the most — moving from 8×8 single-resource to a richer Probe 2 environment is a substantive design change, not a parameter sweep. B is between.

*Risk of latent collapse on the substrate.* A > B > C. Candidate A has the lowest per-step bit-content and is the most likely to land in the regime where free bits dominates and z_t carries little information; C is the safest from that failure mode but its safety comes from being closer to published-precedent territory.

### What the research cannot settle

Several questions will not be answered by literature search and Kind should accept that going in. The numeric thresholds — minimum grid size, action-space cardinality, stochasticity rate, latent dimension at which the RSSM collapses on a small environment — are not characterised in the literature for environments at the scale Kind wants. Kind's environment is smaller than published precedent; the closest precedents (MiniGrid 5×5, Animal-AI single-room arenas) were not used to train RSSM-class agents in their published forms. The detection claim of Probe 4 — that an unmarked builder perturbation can be statistically distinguished from internal stochasticity by an RSSM through likelihood signature alone — is novel; the relevant adjacent work (developmental-robotics caregiver scaffolding, agency-detection in active inference) provides ingredients but not a precedent, and Kind should expect to learn this from running Probe 4 rather than from reading about it.

The phenomenological and enactive commitments — that perception be from somewhere, that the agent have a perspective, that there be enough action variety to support structural coupling — translate into the design as qualitative constraints (partial view, ≥3 actions, non-trivial environmental contingency) rather than numerical thresholds. The literature speaks at the level of recipe, not at the level of parameter. That is honest; the alternative would be invented precedent.

The choice between A, B, and C is the user's. The research's contribution is to frame each as a coherent set of choices with their failure modes and their fidelities to commitment named. A is the choice that most embodies "tiny environment producing one unambiguous surprise"; C is the choice that most embodies "capacity-over-exercise"; B is the compromise. None of the three is required by the substrate or by the literature; each is defensible.
