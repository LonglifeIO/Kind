[canonical] **Ephemeral objects with characteristic lifecycles** are learnable when their births and deaths are not i.i.d. noise, but are tied to a latent process such as age, phase, or local context. ## Curiosity after mastery and the reset-camping failure mode

[canonical] Prediction-error curiosity, disagreement-based curiosity, RND, and learning-progress formulations are not interchangeable. ICM scores the error of predicting the consequences of one's own actions in a learned feature space and explicitly ignores aspects of the environment that cannot affect the agent. RND instead scores novelty as the error of matching a fixed random target network on observations. Disagreement methods estimate uncertainty from variance across multiple forward predictors. Learning-progress models prefer situations that are neither too simple nor too unpredictable, because those are the situations where improvement is possible. citeturn19view0turn7search0turn14view0turn20view0

[constructed] That difference matters for Io. Because the strongest existing empirical finding is a sharply learned self/world boundary, Io is already unusually prepared to benefit from enrichments that thicken the family of *self-caused* transitions. ICM-like feature logic would privilege that family by construction, but your actor is already fixed on disagreement, not ICM. So the best design move is environmental: introduce dynamics where actions create rich, modelable consequences, and let disagreement discover them. That plays to the current agent's strength without adding any new actor input or reward machinery. citeturn19view0turn14view0turn15view0

[canonical] The literature is consistent on one point: good intrinsic motivation lives on the border between the already mastered and the utterly chaotic. Oudeyer's learning-progress account formalizes this as attraction to domains that are neither too predictable nor too unpredictable. Pathak's work and later noisy-TV papers show that raw surprise can be hijacked by irreducible stochasticity. Methods that estimate epistemic rather than aleatoric uncertainty try to solve exactly that problem by removing uncertainty that remains even after the process is as well learned as it can be. citeturn20view0turn14view3turn9search4

[constructed] In design terms, the distinction you need is this:

A world dynamic is **bad noise** when the conditional uncertainty remains high even after the model has seen the same state enough times. The signature is persistent disagreement or prediction error *without local improvement*. Fixed-clock whole-board resampling, per-step i.i.d. teleports, and high-turn-probability random walkers all lean this way. citeturn14view3turn16view0

A world dynamic is **good unfolding structure** when local uncertainty shrinks with experience, but new uncertainty keeps appearing because the process unfolds over state, time, or composition. Examples are a moving object with mostly deterministic motion but occasional state-dependent turns, a hidden timer that periodically causes observable bursts, or a slow spatial field that drifts and changes where resources regrow. In those cases, the agent has somewhere to keep learning because the process is compressible, but not exhaustible in one brief pass. citeturn14view0turn20view0turn21view0

[constructed] Reset-camping is therefore not just an implementation nuisance. It reveals a design law for World v2: **do not hand out global novelty on a schedule that can be collected while inert**. If novelty enters exogenously, it should do so as slow, local, history-bearing change rather than as a regular board-wide replacement. Otherwise Io can rationally prefer stillness on the most informative-looking square. citeturn16view0turn14view3

## A concrete candidate set for World v2

[constructed] Every proposal below respects the stated constraints: no reward, no installed preference, no new policy inputs, no internal-state contingency, no observation labels, and nothing mandatory. Each arrives as ordinary world dynamics. The order matters because z=16 is small.

**Persistent terrain with rare rearrangement.** Start with 6-10 impassable cells arranged as one or two stable walls and a narrow corridor. Let one small segment reconfigure only rarely, for example by a state-independent hazard of about 0.002-0.005 per step, or by a long hidden local clock. The thing Io must represent is stable topology plus occasional topology change. This is the cheapest addition and the best first test because it strengthens place-structure and action-consequence geometry without multiplying object bookkeeping. The main confound is overconstraining locomotion; if the walls cut the map into trivial loops, curiosity will collapse into path repetition. citeturn17view4turn17view2

**One autonomous drifter with inertial motion and contact response.** Add a single persistent object that occupies one cell, keeps a heading, advances one cell per step, bounces off edges and walls, and changes heading only with a low hazard, around 0.01-0.03 per step. If Io walks into it, the object is displaced or its heading flips according to a fixed collision rule; nothing is consumed. This is pure world physics and directly enriches the already-measured self/world boundary, because now some world changes are self-caused but *not identical to self-motion*. The confound is making it too stochastic: if the turn hazard is high, the object becomes a mini noisy TV. With one mover, z=16 is probably enough; with two movers, I would expect capacity strain. citeturn11search5turn15view0turn18view0

**A hidden-phase bloom process.** Choose 2 fixed cells that each run a 4-phase internal clock not explicitly visible to Io. Every 10-16 steps, phase advances; on one phase, the cell emits a short-lived visible bloom into its Moore neighborhood for 2-3 steps, slightly alters passability for 1-2 steps, or changes local regrowth in a patterned way. Optional contact rule: stepping onto the source delays or advances the phase by exactly one step. Prediction now requires carrying an unobserved timer in latent state. This is the cleanest way to add hidden state without labels. The failure mode is overlong period or too many asynchronous clocks; then the process is present but too thinly sampled for a small flat RSSM. citeturn18view0turn21view0turn17view4

**A short delayed causal chain.** Add one small propagation path, such as a 3-4 cell "waveguide," whose membership is only discoverable through dynamics. When Io steps on the source cell, a wave moves along the path with a one-step delay per cell and briefly alters the terminal cell after 2-4 steps. The terminal change can be a transient blocker, a transient opening, or a local bloom. This gives you relational and delayed structure without installing meaning. What must be represented is not a label but a causal dependence over time and space. The confound is length: beyond a short delay, the chain becomes a memory tax rather than an enrichment. Keep exactly one chain at first. citeturn14view0turn17view4

**A slow global context with patchy resources.** Keep resources, but stop making them the only novelty source. Introduce a weak 2-state or 4-state exogenous cycle over roughly 48-96 steps that slowly shifts a spatial field across the board. Under that field, regrowth is, say, 3-5x higher than elsewhere; outside it, regrowth remains possible but sparse. The field can drift one column or row every 12-24 steps. This makes scarcity epistemically valuable because food becomes something like *weather* rather than confetti. The confound is crowding out everything else: if the field dominates observation and occupancy statistics, Io may simply become a patch tracker. This is why I would add it only after terrain, drifter, and one hidden process are already established. citeturn20view0turn14view0turn21view0

[constructed] The recommended ordering is: **terrain first, then one drifter, then one hidden-phase process, then one short causal chain, then the slow resource field**. That order is not cosmetic. It moves from cheap spatial structure, to richer self-caused transitions, to hidden state, to delayed causality, to multi-timescale context. Each step asks the latent to do one new kind of work rather than all kinds at once. citeturn17view4turn21view0turn15view0

## Capacity discipline and what to add only later if ever

[constructed] On Q7, I do **not** think the first three additions I recommended require more than the current substrate, if they are introduced one at a time and kept singular: one terrain reconfiguration process, one drifter, one hidden-phase source. That package asks the latent to track topology, one persistent moving object, and one local hidden timer. For an 8x8 world, that is ambitious but still plausible for h=200, z=16, K=5. citeturn11search5turn18view0turn17view4

[constructed] The main capacity risks are cumulative. Risk rises sharply when you combine **multiple autonomous movers**, **multiple asynchronous hidden clocks**, **long delayed chains**, and **fast exogenous cycles**. Then the model must simultaneously represent self-location, object identity, velocities, timer phases, delayed dependencies, and slow context. In flat small latents, that often shows up as one of three failure signatures: posterior underuse or collapse of the stochastic state, chronic prediction error that never settles, or ensemble disagreement that behaves like noise instead of knowledge gap. Hierarchical and structured world-model work exists precisely because multi-timescale and interaction-rich domains strain flat monolithic models. citeturn21view0turn17view4turn13search6
