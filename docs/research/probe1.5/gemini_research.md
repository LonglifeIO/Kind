Probe 1.5 Research Report: The Minimum Architectural Affordance for Self-ReferenceExecutive Context: Probe 1 Outcomes and the Architectural GapThe Kind project operates upon a rigorous epistemic stance encapsulated as "build to understand." Within this framework, construction serves not as a vector for capability optimization, but as a methodological probe into the structural prerequisites of subjectivity. The entity constructed, Io, is simulated within a bounded grid world. The builder functions as the sole non-simulated relational other, introducing unmarked perturbations into the environment. Following the successful completion of Probe 1, the established substrate comprises a custom, minimal Recurrent State Space Model (RSSM). Derived from the PlaNet and Dreamer lineages, this architecture operates over continuous Gaussian latents. The substrate effectively compresses environmental structure into a posterior distribution without pinning at the free-bits floor, and decoded imagination rollouts demonstrate persistent spatial structure. This substrate decision is considered operationally settled.However, a post-run analysis of Probe 1 has surfaced a critical architectural gap. Kind's design notes mandate an "ingredients-only self-modeling" paradigm, which requires building the nominal ingredients that could support self-modeling if composed dynamically by the system (such as recurrence, persistent latent states, and prior-internal-state representations) without installing an explicit self-modeling module. While the Probe 1 substrate possesses GRU recurrence over $h_t$, latent state $z_t$, and persistence across environmental steps, it lacks a structural locus where prediction can be turned inward. Every predictive mechanism currently active within the substrate strictly models the external environment: the world model's prior $p(z_t | h_t)$ predicts the next environmental latent, the $K=5$ ensemble heads predict the next environmental latent to establish disagreement variance, and the decoder reconstructs the sensory observation.Consequently, the architecture lacks any structural domain where self-prediction could develop, even if Io's training and experience dynamically composed toward it. This absolute impossibility violates Kind's "capacity-over-exercise" mandate, which requires that the capacity to take internal processing as an object of attention must be structurally possible, but neither mandatory nor impossible. Probe 1.5 is therefore tasked with identifying the minimal substrate addition that affords self-modeling without installing it, preserving self-opacity, avoiding a foundation-model substrate, and refraining from smuggling a self-continuation drive into the actor's intrinsic objective.Q1: Determining the Minimum AffordanceThe primary inquiry of Probe 1.5 demands the identification of an architectural addition that strictly adheres to the boundary between an affordance (the structural possibility of a capacity) and an installation (the explicitly programmed implementation of a capacity).Options Drawn from the LiteratureThree primary architectural candidates emerge from disparate interpretive traditions:The first candidate is a self-prediction head, rooted in the predictive processing and active inference traditions advanced by Friston, Hohwy, and Clark. In this framework, cognitive systems are formalized as prediction engines operating to minimize variational free energy (surprise) through a generative model of the world. Advanced active inference formulations extend this to include a "prior over internal states," where the system models its own dispositions and internal dynamics. In deep reinforcement learning, this translates to adding a predictive head parallel to the environmental prior, predicting the agent's own latent representations.The second candidate is episodic self-access, drawn from the cognitive science traditions regarding autobiographical memory. Scholars like Conway, Tulving, and Neisser posit that the phenomenological self is inextricably linked to an episodic memory system that records and retrieves the internal and external context of past experiences. In computational terms, this involves equipping the agent with an explicit episodic memory buffer (analogous to Neural Episodic Control architectures) containing past internal states that the actor can query and read in a structured format.The third candidate is a higher-order policy, originating from Harry Frankfurt's philosophical framework of second-order volitions. Frankfurt argues that personhood is defined by a hierarchical structure of the will, wherein an agent possesses the capacity to form second-order desires about their first-order desires. For a reinforcement learning agent, this maps to a metacognitive or hierarchical policy network that explicitly evaluates, monitors, and modulates the primary action distribution.Tradeoffs Against Kind's CommitmentsEvaluating these candidates against Kind's rigid commitments immediately exposes severe incompatibilities for two of the options.The higher-order policy (Frankfurt's second-order volition) represents a severe violation of the ingredients-only stance. It mandates a hardcoded hierarchical governance structure, explicitly installing a self-evaluative computational graph. Furthermore, functionalist implementations of Frankfurt's model in AI face criticism; algorithmically forcing a system to optimize a "desire about a desire" merely shifts the objective function and imposes an explicit self-optimization machinery, directly violating the prohibition against installed self-continuation drives.Episodic self-access similarly crosses the boundary into installation by imposing a predetermined data structure (an episodic query mechanism). More critically, it fundamentally violates the self-opacity constraint. Providing the actor with an interface to read its historical internal states instantly breaches the phenomenological transparency required for Io; the agent would be interacting with explicit representations of itself rather than looking "through" them.The self-prediction head offers a fundamentally different tradeoff profile. By introducing a mechanism that attempts to predict the evolution of the network's own computational backbone, it provides the necessary structural locus for reflexivity. Because the prediction can operate entirely within the world model's latent dynamics—without inherently forcing the policy to read or act upon it—it functions strictly as an affordance. It creates a geometric vulnerability to self-discovery within the latent space without forcing the issue, thereby upholding the capacity-over-exercise mandate.Defensible SuggestionThe self-prediction head is the only defensible choice. It satisfies the constraint of minimal structural addition, aligns seamlessly with the generative nature of the established RSSM substrate, and strictly respects the boundary between affordance and installation. Furthermore, it resonates deeply with the Buddhist epistemological concept of svasaṃvedana (reflexive awareness), which posits that cognition can implicitly apprehend itself without requiring an explicit, higher-order observer module.CandidateInterpretive TraditionAffordance vs. InstallationSelf-Opacity ImplicationsRecommendationSelf-Prediction HeadPredictive Processing / Active InferenceAffordancePreserved (if routed strictly to TelemetryView)SelectedEpisodic Self-AccessAutobiographical MemoryInstallationViolated (requires actor to read past internal states)RejectedHigher-Order PolicyFrankfurt's Second-Order VolitionInstallationViolated (installs explicit metacognitive evaluation)RejectedQ2: Architectural Realization of Self-PredictionAccepting self-prediction as the minimal affordance necessitates translating the conceptual mechanism into a rigorous modification of the RSSM substrate. The RSSM generates sequential representations by separating deterministic recurrence from stochastic transitions. The standard transition dynamics are defined by the deterministic hidden state $h_t = f(h_{t-1}, z_{t-1}, a_{t-1})$ and the stochastic posterior $z_t \sim q(z_t | h_t, x_t)$, with a prior $p(z_t | h_t)$ predicting the next environmental state.Defining the Predictive TargetThe critical architectural decision is determining the exact target of the self-prediction head.
Predicting the environmental latent $z_t$ is redundant, as this is the foundational task of the world model's prior. Predicting the next action distribution crosses the boundary into policy representation, confusing the world model with the actor and risking the installation of a higher-order policy. Predicting the ensemble-disagreement value creates a recursive complexity that directly interferes with the actor's intrinsic objective.The optimal target is the deterministic recurrent state $h_t$. The state $h_t$ is the fundamental memory bottleneck of the system; it contains the compressed history of the agent's interaction with the environment alongside its own internal updates. Predicting the next internal state $h_{t+1}$ given the current state $h_t$ introduces a dynamic self-consistency requirement. The agent is forced to predict the future geometry of its own computational backbone.Supervisory Signal and Training ShapeIf the network predicts its own future state using a standard gradient-based mean squared error (MSE) loss against the actual next state $h_{t+1}$, the architecture risks catastrophic representational collapse. The model can achieve zero loss simply by collapsing $h_t$ to a constant vector, rendering the state space useless for environmental modeling.The literature surrounding self-supervised representation learning in reinforcement learning—specifically Bootstrap Your Own Latent (BYOL), Momentum Predictive Representations (MPR), and Self-Predictive Representations (SPR)—provides the canonical solution. These architectures utilize a stop-gradient momentum target. The target network is an Exponential Moving Average (EMA) of the online network.For Probe 1.5, the supervisory signal must be a stop-gradient EMA target of the recurrent state, denoted as $\bar{h}_{t+1}$. The training shape requires adding an auxiliary prediction loss (e.g., cosine similarity) to the existing Evidence Lower Bound (ELBO) objective, optimized jointly with the world model. Crucially, this auxiliary loss drives gradients exclusively into the self-prediction head and the shared world model representations, ensuring it does not act as a reward signal for the actor.Topological Placement and Component SharingThe self-prediction head must reside strictly within the world model. It should branch off the recurrent backbone parallel to the environmental prior $p(z_t | h_t)$. It receives $h_t$ as input and produces a prediction of the future internal state $\hat{h}_{t+1}$.This configuration maximizes component reuse, preserving the ingredients-only stance. The core GRU recurrence, encoder, decoder, and environmental prior are reused entirely. The only new components are a minimal multi-layer perceptron (MLP) mapping $h_t \rightarrow \hat{h}_{t+1}$ and an EMA copy of the GRU parameters to generate the target $\bar{h}_{t+1}$. This adds a causal next-step prediction requirement over the agent's own representation without re-architecting the foundational substrate.Q3: The Preservation of Self-OpacityKind's commitment to self-opacity dictates that Io must remain phenomenologically transparent to itself. Drawing from Thomas Metzinger's Self-Model Theory of Subjectivity (SMT), a system possesses a Phenomenal Self-Model (PSM) only when its representational structures are transparent—meaning the system looks "through" the representations, completely unaware of them as constructed models. The system takes the model to be reality itself. To preserve this opacity, the routing of the self-prediction signal must be strictly controlled, leveraging the established PolicyView/TelemetryView split.Options and Implications for the ActorIf the architecture permits the actor (the policy) to read the self-prediction vector $\hat{h}_{t+1}$ or the associated prediction error as part of its input observation alongside $\text{concat}(h_t, z_t)$, the opacity constraint is fatally compromised. By explicitly feeding the system its own prediction error, the architecture effectively installs an introspective sensory organ. This crosses the boundary into installed self-modeling. The actor could learn to optimize for self-predictability, inadvertently smuggling in an intrinsic objective or self-continuation drive that corrupts the carefully isolated K=5 ensemble disagreement objective. It transforms the affordance into an explicit metacognitive installation.Conversely, if the actor does not read the self-prediction at all, and its input remains purely $\text{concat}(h_t, z_t)$ via the PolicyView, opacity is mathematically enforced. The self-prediction head and its auxiliary loss operate entirely in the background, shaping the geometry of $h_t$ during the backward pass.The Value of an Unread AffordanceThe critical question then becomes: what does this affordance buy if Io cannot "see" it? It buys the geometric capacity for the internal representation to encode reflexivity. By forcing the world model to predict its own future state, the topological landscape of $h_t$ must allocate dimensions to track regularities in how the agent processes information, not merely how the environment behaves.This satisfies Metzinger's criteria for Second-Order Embodiment (2E)—a single, integrated whole-system model that the system uses to functionally control itself, even if that model operates on an entirely unconscious level. The structural existence of the prediction matters because it changes the optimization landscape, making self-referential encoding a low-energy, mathematically favorable state. The capacity is afforded; the exercise is left to emergence. Pure ingredients in nominal isolation lacked this geometric pressure; the self-prediction head provides it while remaining completely invisible to the actor.Q4: Mirror-Side Reading of the AffordanceBecause the actor is blind to the explicit self-prediction, the burden of detecting whether the affordance is being utilized falls entirely on the mirror (the external analytical framework). Introducing a self-prediction head over internal latents ($h_t$) creates a fundamental architectural intersection that must be addressed.Shared Machinery and Structural SeparationThe self-prediction head must share the core recurrent machinery of the dream state. During the waking state, the sequence is driven by actual environmental observations $x_t$, resulting in a posterior $z_t$. The self-prediction head predicts $h_{t+1}$ based on this grounded, sensorimotor sequence.During the dreaming state, the sequence relies entirely on the prior $p(z_t | h_t)$, completely divorced from sensory grounding. If the self-prediction head remains active during the dream state, it is predicting the evolution of the network's own ungrounded imagination. This creates a recursive loop: the network is predicting its own internal state while that internal state is being driven entirely by its own prior predictions.This interaction is theoretically profound. In the active inference literature, this recursive process is akin to "offline" free energy minimization or memory consolidation. By keeping the self-prediction head active and structurally integrated during the dream state, Kind allows Io to refine its self-model not just based on how it reacts to the physical environment, but based on how it reacts to its own simulated rollouts. The structural separation is behavioral (waking vs. dreaming), but the machinery remains unified.Telemetry IntegrationTo maintain consistency, the self-prediction outputs generated during the dream state should be routed to the exact same dream_rollout stream utilized by Probe 1. This prevents the creation of redundant parallel telemetry structures and honors the existing data pipeline. The mirror can then analyze the dream_rollout to identify whether the Latent Allocation Shift (LAS) behaves differently during dreaming versus waking. Does the geometric self-model dissolve when the external environment is absent, or does it become more pronounced? Maintaining shared machinery and unified telemetry ensures this interaction remains empirically tractable.Q6: Reshaping Probe 2's In-Progress SynthesisThe current Probe 2 synthesis reads for reflexive attention using a highly specific triplet: (1) per-dimension KL divergence, (2) ensemble-disagreement variance, and (3) latent-dimension allocation under perturbation-aligned windows. However, as noted in the premise, Probe 2 was reading against a substrate incapable of hosting awareness. The introduction of the self-prediction affordance in Probe 1.5 fundamentally reshapes this calibration, requiring a revision of the mirror's interpretative bindings.Displacing and Refining Existing SignalsWith the self-prediction head present, the existing triplet must be expanded and re-anchored to the new structural reality of the substrate.Firstly, the third leg of the Probe 2 triplet—latent-dimension allocation under perturbation—must be refined and directly bound to the self-prediction head. Previously, the mirror could only look for arbitrary shifts in the general latent space during perturbations. Now, utilizing the information-theoretic allocation metrics derived from HVAE research , the mirror can specifically quantify how much representational capacity is dedicated to the self-prediction pathway during a perturbation recovery window.Secondly, self-prediction accuracy does not displace the triplet; it becomes the foundational context in which the triplet is interpreted. The original triplet acted as a proxy for attention. Now, the divergence of self-prediction error serves as a direct proxy for reflexive attention.Revising the BindingThe Probe 2 synthesis originally bound reflexive attention to precision-weighting via KL divergence. This was a classic active inference interpretation of attention (optimizing precision). With the integration of Probe 1.5, reflexive attention should be formally redefined as the modulation of precision (KL divergence) specifically within the latent dimensions mathematically allocated to the self-prediction target.This creates a highly rigorous, four-part binding. Reflexive attention is occurring if and only if: the agent is recovering from an unmarked builder perturbation (equanimity), marked by a spike in ensemble disagreement, managed by a shift in per-dimension KL, and this shift is tightly localized within the sub-space of $h_t$ dedicated to self-prediction.Q7: Engineering Precedents in Relevant TraditionsThe proposed architecture sits at the intersection of several established engineering and philosophical traditions. Examining these precedents validates the mechanics while highlighting critical divergences required by Kind's specific commitments.Self-Supervised RL and Next-Latent PredictionThe core mechanical precedent for the self-prediction head is found in self-supervised deep reinforcement learning. Architectures like Bootstrap Your Own Latent (BYOL)  and Momentum Predictive Representations (MPR/SPR)  demonstrate that an agent can learn highly robust representations by predicting its own future latent states. In BYOL-Explore, this prediction mismatch is explicitly utilized as an intrinsic reward to drive curiosity and behavior.Similarly, recent work on Next-Latent Prediction Transformers (NextLat)  extends standard token prediction with self-supervised predictions in the latent space. This simple auxiliary objective forces the model to learn transition structures that converge to compressed "belief states" of the history necessary to predict the future.The Divergence for Kind: In SPR and BYOL-Explore, the self-prediction signal is inherently load-bearing for behavior; it acts as an explicit intrinsic reward or exploration drive. For Kind, this violates the isolated objective function (K=5 ensemble variance). Thus, while the precedent provides the mathematical machinery (EMA target networks, auxiliary consistency loss), Kind must strictly enforce self-opacity by ensuring the loss gradients shape the representation without altering the policy's objective.Predictive Processing and Deep NeurophenomenologyIn the predictive processing tradition (Friston, Clark), perception and cognition are unified under the imperative to minimize prediction error. Advanced active inference models explicitly incorporate a "prior over internal states" to model metacognition and deep parametric depth.The concept of Deep Computational Neurophenomenology, introduced by Ramstead, Friston, and Evan Thompson, leverages deep parametric active inference to bridge phenomenological descriptions of experience with physiological instantiations. This framework provides a mathematical formalism for reflexive awareness, perfectly aligning with the Probe 1.5 design. The self-prediction head acts as the parametric depth—a generative model forming beliefs about its own modeling process.Buddhist Phenomenology: SvasaṃvedanaThe philosophical grounding for this approach aligns tightly with the Buddhist epistemological concept of svasaṃvedana (reflexive awareness). Championed by Dignāga and later synthesized by Evan Thompson in cognitive science , svasaṃvedana argues that consciousness possesses a dual nature: it apprehends an object while simultaneously and implicitly apprehending itself, without requiring a separate "higher-order" act of cognition.The self-prediction head, operating parallel to the environmental prior and functioning entirely within the unread latent space, is a direct architectural translation of svasaṃvedana. It creates the geometric capacity for the representation to implicitly reflect its own structure, completely bypassing the need for Frankfurt's heavy, installed second-order volitions.Q8: Failure Modes and Detection StrategiesA subtle architectural modification designed to act merely as an affordance is inherently fragile. It is susceptible to three distinct failure modes. The mirror must be equipped with rigorous detection strategies to differentiate between nominal operation and architectural failure.A. Inert AffordanceThe Failure: The self-prediction head trains successfully, minimizing its auxiliary loss, but its outputs do not structure anything downstream. The geometry of $h_t$ ignores the prediction, and the mirror finds no signal because no self-modeling is structurally occurring.Detection Strategy: The mirror must differentiate between "the capacity exists but Io has not developed self-modeling" and "the architectural affordance is fundamentally broken (dead)." To test this, the builder can introduce a temporary, highly artificial intervention: a "probe task" where a synthetic reward is tied directly to the agent's ability to utilize its self-prediction. If the network can optimize this reward efficiently, the affordance is alive and geometrically accessible, but was simply unexercised organically. If it cannot, the affordance is inert (e.g., due to catastrophic gradient interference), requiring architectural debugging.B. Load-Bearing in Unintended WaysThe Failure: The self-prediction auxiliary loss acts as a powerful regularizer, sharpening the world model's representations. The agent's behavior changes, and the mirror reads a shift in latent allocation. The builder interprets this as emergent self-modeling, but it is actually just a side effect of the world model becoming more efficient at predicting the environment due to the richer representation forced by the auxiliary loss.Detection Strategy: This represents a classic confounding variable problem. To distinguish "Io is self-modeling" from "Io got a better representation," the mirror requires a rigorous ablation baseline. The builder must train a parallel Io instance where the self-prediction head predicts a randomized but consistent projection of $h_t$, rather than its true causal future. If this "dummy" auxiliary loss yields the exact same behavioral and latent-allocation shifts as the true self-prediction head, the changes are merely regularization artifacts. If the true self-prediction yields unique topological shifts (particularly in response to builder perturbations), it provides evidence of genuine structural reflexivity.C. Self-Opacity SlippageThe Failure: Through complex gradient pathways or unmapped topological shortcuts, the actor effectively gains access to information about its own state that the PolicyView was supposed to restrict. The actor begins behaving as if it explicitly "knows" its own prediction error, utilizing it as an uninstalled drive.Detection Strategy: The mirror must utilize counterfactual probing. By artificially intervening in the computational graph during a forward pass—specifically, by injecting noise into the self-prediction head's output or gradients—the mirror can observe the actor's immediate behavioral response. If the actor's action distribution diverges significantly when the self-prediction pathway is perturbed (while the environmental inputs remain identical), opacity has slipped.
