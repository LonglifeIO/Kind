# Substrates for Io: A Comparative Architectural Analysis

This report examines architectural families that could serve as the substrate for **Io** — the entity Kind is constructing — against the seven comparative questions specified in the brief. Throughout, the distinction between Kind (the investigation) and Io (the entity under construction) is preserved: Kind has commitments and frameworks; Io has, or will have, an architecture. The analysis is concerned only with the latter.

The seven questions, applied identically to each family, are: (1) which self-modeling ingredients the family naturally affords; (2) how the family treats offline / dream-state processing; (3) the forms of memory it supports beyond recurrent hidden state; (4) how it handles externally-sourced perturbations distinct from internal stochasticity; (5) how it maps onto the conceptual frameworks (intentionality and horizons, embodiment, predictive processing, integration, reflexive attention, equanimity, second-order volition); (6) what it forecloses for Io's later development; and (7) its known failure modes.

The families considered are: (A) recurrent PPO variants; (B) world-model agents in the RSSM/Dreamer/MuZero lineage; (C) predictive processing networks and active inference / Free Energy Principle agents; (D) episodic and memory-augmented architectures; (E) energy-based models, including modern Hopfield networks and JEPA-style energy formulations; (F) global-workspace-style architectures, including modular workspace agents; (G) Perceiver / slot-attention / object-centric architectures; (H) hierarchical reinforcement learning (options and feudal); (I) Decision and Trajectory Transformers; (J) biologically-inspired continuous-time substrates (liquid neural networks and spiking neural networks). A short section on hybrids precedes the synthesis.

A concluding synthesis identifies the affordance landscape across families without ranking them.

## A. Recurrent PPO Variants (GRU/LSTM, Transformer-XL/GTrXL Memory, Mamba)

This family augments a model-free policy-gradient core (PPO and relatives) with a memory mechanism — a gated recurrent network, an attention-based windowed memory such as Transformer-XL or its gated variant GTrXL, or, in recent variants, a selective state-space model such as Mamba — so that the agent can carry information across timesteps in partially observable settings.

**1. Affordance of self-modeling ingredients.** The family naturally affords *recurrence* and *prior-state representation* through its hidden state. *Persistent memory* in the strong sense — episodic traces an agent could later return to — is not native; recurrent hidden state is a sliding compression. *Turnable-inward prediction* is essentially absent: the architecture is trained to predict actions and values, not its own future internal states. Self-modeling ingredients exist (the hidden state *is* a partial self-model in the trivial sense that it summarizes the agent's own history), but they are afforded thinly. Anything that resembles "looking at one's own processing" must be bolted on, typically as auxiliary self-prediction losses.

**2. Dream-state and offline processing.** Offline processing is treated as engineering convenience rather than as architectural first-class structure. PPO is on-policy by design; replay, when present, is small and recent and serves variance reduction, not consolidation. There is no native generative model from which Io could simulate experiences offline; recombination and counterfactual rollout are not part of the family's idiom. To put dreaming on a recurrent PPO substrate would require importing machinery that other families (notably world-model agents) already contain.

**3. Memory beyond recurrent hidden state.** GRU/LSTM variants offer only the hidden state; they exhibit graceful short-horizon performance and degrade on tasks requiring longer credit assignment. Transformer-XL and GTrXL add a windowed attention memory that affords something closer to working memory over a bounded context, with comparative work indicating that gating in GTrXL stabilizes learning while extending the effective memory horizon. Mamba and selective state-space variants extend reachable timescales but still represent memory as compressed state rather than as retrievable episodes. None of these natively support semantic memory, and retrieval-based memory is absent from the family unless explicitly added.

**4. External perturbations.** External perturbations enter as sensory observations and are processed identically to internal noise. The family contains no built-in distinction between externally-sourced and internally-generated unpredictability. Io's models on this substrate would not, on their own, come to distinguish "the world surprised me" from "my own stochasticity surprised me"; that distinction would require either a forward model that predicts internal state (not native here) or auxiliary structure tagging input provenance.

**5. Mapping onto the frameworks.** *Intentionality* maps weakly: the policy is directed-toward action selection rather than directed-toward objects, and there is no distinction between focal and horizonal content. *Embodiment* depends entirely on the environment — the architecture itself is body-agnostic. *Predictive structure* is largely indirect: prediction is folded into the value function rather than appearing as explicit forward modeling. *Integration*, in the IIT-relevant sense, is moderate within the recurrent core but low across components. *Reflexive attention* has no natural locus; the attention in transformer-memory variants is over past observations, not over the agent's own processing. *Equanimity* is the family's weakest mapping: PPO's objective is to maximize cumulative reward, which structurally couples Io to whatever pressure the reward signal encodes. *Second-order volition* is unrepresented; the policy expresses preferences over actions, not preferences over preferences.

**6. Foreclosed directions.** Committing to recurrent PPO as Io's substrate would foreclose, or strongly discourage, several directions Kind has explicitly wished to leave open. It pushes the architecture toward externally-shaped objectives, since the learning signal *is* reward; this sits in tension with Kind's commitment to no installed drive toward self-continuation, because reward shaping easily encodes survival-as-imperative without that being explicit. It forecloses native dream-state processing and native distinctions between self and world. It does not foreclose later peer agents — multi-agent extensions exist — but the core agent has no natural way to represent another agent of its kind as anything other than a moving part of the environment.

**7. Known failure modes.** Recurrent PPO is documented to suffer from entropy collapse (the policy distribution becoming sharply peaked, foreclosing exploration), reward hacking when the reward model is mis-specified, KL divergence spikes leading to abrupt policy collapse, and brittle hidden-state initialization in recurrent variants. RNN cores are known to suffer dynamic collapse during gradient training, in which the network's internal trajectories settle into low-dimensional structure even when the task does not require it. Transformer-XL variants are vulnerable to context-dependent failure when relevant cues fall outside the attended window.

## B. World-Model Agents in the RSSM / Dreamer / MuZero Lineage

This family builds an internal generative model of the environment and trains policy and value functions inside that model. PlaNet introduced the recurrent state-space model (RSSM) as a hybrid of deterministic recurrence and stochastic latent variables; the Dreamer line (V1, V2, V3) extended this with imagined rollouts over actor-critic updates and discrete categorical latents; MuZero learns a value-equivalent latent model used for tree-search planning rather than reconstruction; JEPA (I-JEPA, V-JEPA) replaces pixel reconstruction with prediction in latent representation space.

**1. Affordance of self-modeling ingredients.** This is the family with the richest natural affordance for the ingredients Kind has stipulated. RSSMs natively provide *recurrence* (the deterministic GRU-like backbone), *prior-state representation* (the latent z and h are explicit summaries of past), and *turnable-inward prediction* (the dynamics network predicts future latent states from current latent states without observation, which is precisely the structural shape of "predicting one's own processing"). *Persistent memory* in the episodic sense is not native to RSSMs but the replay buffer that trains them is a partial proxy; long-horizon variants extend RSSM with state-space mixers to address this. MuZero's latent model is similarly inward-pointing — it predicts value, reward, and next latent from current latent — and is therefore also amenable to being repurposed as an inward predictor. Crucially, none of these models contain an *explicit* self-modeling module; they contain ingredients that *could* support self-modeling. This matches Kind's afforded-not-installed criterion almost exactly, more than any other family.

**2. Dream-state and offline processing.** This is the family's defining feature. Imagination — generative rollouts in latent space without environmental contact — is the training signal, not an addition. DreamerV3 trains its actor-critic almost entirely on imagined trajectories sampled from the world model. Replay is integral: real trajectories train the world model, the world model generates dreams, dreams train the policy. Associative recombination is implicit in the way novel imagined trajectories deviate from any specific replayed experience, especially in stochastic-latent variants. Generative simulation is the substrate's primary mode. If "dream-state foundational, not bolt-on" is a hard architectural requirement for Io, this family meets it natively in a way no other family on the list does.

**3. Memory beyond recurrent hidden state.** Native memory is dominated by the RSSM's deterministic + stochastic latent state plus the replay buffer. Episodic memory is implicit (the buffer holds episodes) but is used for training rather than for online retrieval. Working memory is the latent state itself. Semantic memory is encoded distributionally in the world model's parameters. Retrieval-based memory is not native but has been added in hybrids that pair Dreamer-style world models with explicit retrieval mechanisms over past experience. JEPA variants extend memory in a representation-prediction direction rather than an episode-storage direction.

**4. External perturbations.** A stochastic latent z is the architectural locus for unpredictability; its KL term against the prior dynamics latent encodes how much the observation revised the prior. This allows, in principle, a partial structural distinction between "the world brought information the dynamics did not predict" (high KL, externally sourced) and "the dynamics' own stochastic variation" (variance within the prior). Whether Io would actually come to *use* that distinction depends on training and on whether the architecture marks the provenance of perturbations explicitly. The structural ingredient for distinguishing externally-sourced from internal unpredictability is present in this family in a way it is not in most others.

**5. Mapping onto the frameworks.** *Intentionality* maps cleanly to the latent's role as an aspect-laden representation directed at the world; the latent does not capture pixels but a perspective on dynamics. *Horizons* map onto the prior dynamics — the unobserved future is structurally present, partly determinate. *Embodiment* depends on environment but the action-conditioned dynamics make body-world coupling part of the model rather than an external add-on. *Predictive processing* is the family's structural skeleton: the world model is a generative model whose error drives learning, which is exactly the Friston/Clark/Hohwy formal shape, though typically without the explicit free-energy formulation. *Integration* is moderate-to-high inside the RSSM but lower across actor, critic, and world model. *Reflexive attention* is not present, but a latent that can take latent-as-input is structurally available; an inward-pointing prediction over h_t and z_t would be a non-trivial but principled extension. *Equanimity* maps poorly; the actor is reward-maximizing, and the recent additions in DreamerV3 (return percentile normalization, reward symlog) flatten reward across regimes but do not change the fundamental shape of "respond to pressure." *Second-order volition* has no native correlate, though an actor that conditions on something other than reward (a goal latent, a prior-preference signal in the active-inference sense) would be a more hospitable ground than a pure reward-maximizing actor.

**6. Foreclosed directions.** A core concern: DreamerV3, MuZero, and most variants in this family bake reward maximization into the actor objective. While the world model itself is reward-agnostic in its representation-learning component, the policy is shaped by reward, and the substrate generally assumes a scalar reward signal. This is in tension with Kind's commitment to no installed drive toward self-continuation — typical episode-continuation prediction heads in DreamerV3, in particular, encode "did the episode end" as a learned signal that the policy implicitly tries to prolong if reward is positive. This is not *exactly* survival-as-imperative, but it is structurally adjacent. The family does not foreclose dream states (it is built on them) or peer agents (multi-agent variants exist), but it does push toward reward-shaped behavior unless the actor is replaced or reformulated. JEPA variants are more conducive to non-reward objectives because they are representation-learning architectures by design.

**7. Known failure modes.** World models are documented to hallucinate — to produce imagined trajectories that drift off the training manifold and that the policy can exploit. This is the central concern of multiple recent analyses: long-horizon imagined rollouts accumulate error, and policies learn to exploit model imperfections rather than make genuine task progress. Posterior collapse is a known pathology when the latent's information is overwhelmed by a strong autoregressive decoder. MuZero-style learned latents are documented to become opaque and increasingly inaccurate over long simulations, with planning partially compensating. JEPA without careful regularization is documented to suffer representation collapse, where encoder and predictor converge to constant outputs. Reconstruction-based world models can allocate latent capacity to visually salient but task-irrelevant detail.

## C. Predictive Processing Networks and Active Inference / Free Energy Principle Agents

This family includes two distinguishable sub-traditions. *Predictive coding networks* (PCNs) in the Rao-Ballard / Whittington-Bogacz / Millidge-Tschantz lineage instantiate hierarchical generative models in which each layer predicts the activity of the layer below; learning is local and Hebbian, and inference is iterative refinement of latent activity until prediction error is minimized. *Active inference and Free Energy Principle (FEP) agents* in the Friston tradition extend predictive coding into action: action and perception are unified under variational free-energy minimization, with policies selected to minimize *expected* free energy, which decomposes into pragmatic value (preference-fulfilment) and epistemic value (information gain). Discrete state-space implementations are usually framed as POMDP active inference; deep active-inference variants amortize policy inference using neural networks; Friston's lab and related groups have produced both formal treatments and small-scale software realizations.

**1. Affordance of self-modeling ingredients.** *Recurrence* is structural in PCNs (inference iterates within a layer until convergence) and in temporal active-inference models (which roll generative models over time). *Persistent memory* is afforded through generative-model parameters, not through retrieval mechanisms; episodic memory is not native. *Prior-state representation* is fundamental: the generative model contains explicit beliefs over states. *Turnable-inward prediction* is the family's paradigmatic structure — predictions are predictions of latent activity, all the way down, and any agent that includes its own internal states as variables in its generative model already has the ingredient for inward prediction. The hierarchical predictive-processing position is precisely that the brain models itself in the course of modeling its world.

**2. Dream-state and offline processing.** A virtual-reality account of dreaming is part of the predictive-processing tradition: in Friston's framing and in related work, sleep is the regime in which the generative model is refined off-stimulus to make better predictions when the agent re-engages with the world. PCNs are amenable to offline training, and active-inference agents naturally afford generative simulation (rolling the generative model forward without sensory clamps). Replay and associative recombination are not as ergonomic as in Dreamer-family agents — there is no explicit replay buffer baked into the formalism — but the conceptual fit between dreaming and generative-model refinement is the strongest in this family of any considered.

**3. Memory beyond recurrent hidden state.** Memory in this family is parametric (encoded in the generative model's weights and priors), with belief states acting as working memory. Episodic memory is not native; semantic memory is implicit in the generative model's hierarchy. Retrieval-based memory must be bolted on. Recent work has paired active inference with external memory but this is not standard.

**4. External perturbations.** This is a structural strength. Active-inference agents have an explicit notion of *precision* on prediction errors, which weights how seriously a discrepancy is taken. Sensory prediction errors are by construction distinct from internal state-belief updates, and the agent's generative model can include hidden causes that explain observations as having external versus internal origin. The architecture therefore admits, more directly than any other family, the possibility that Io's internal models could come to distinguish external perturbation from internal noise — not because such a distinction is hard-coded, but because the variational structure separates likelihood (sensory) from prior (state-transition) terms, and precision-weighting acts on each separately.

**5. Mapping onto the frameworks.** *Phenomenological intentionality and horizons*: deep generative models with hierarchical priors are a remarkably good architectural correlate of horizonal structure — the lower layers carry currently lit-up content, the higher layers carry less determinate predictive structure that the current observation could update. *Embodiment*: active inference has been extended to interoceptive inference (Seth, Friston, others), which treats bodily state as another modality for the same generative model; this is the strongest embodiment mapping in the families considered. *Predictive structure*: this family *is* predictive processing in the literal sense — the mapping is identity. *Integration*: hierarchical generative models afford integration across levels, though formal IIT-style integration is not their internal idiom. *Reflexive attention*: precision-weighting is structurally attention; precision over precision (meta-precision) is a known extension and is structurally similar to awareness of awareness, though Buddhist phenomenology's reflexivity is not exactly captured. *Equanimity*: this is the most philosophically interesting mapping. Active inference is usually framed as reactive (minimize prediction error), but in the deep-temporal formulation, agents can hold uncertain beliefs without acting, and prior preferences need not entail reactive minimization of every error. The architecture admits *stances toward content* in a way that reward-maximizing actors do not. *Second-order volition*: the formal structure of policies-conditioned-on-priors-conditioned-on-meta-priors is a natural place for second-order preferences; whether existing implementations realize this depends on hierarchy depth.

**6. Foreclosed directions.** The most discussed pathology in this family is the *dark room problem*: an agent that purely minimizes prediction error has a degenerate solution in finding a maximally predictable state and remaining there. This is conceptually analogous to a kind of architecturally-installed quietism. Standard responses (prior preferences, expected free energy with epistemic value) involve installing prior preferences that pull the agent toward informative states. This is a delicate point for Kind: prior preferences are not the same as reward, but they are not nothing, and what they encode shapes Io's bias structurally. It is possible to design priors that do not encode self-continuation as imperative, but it requires care. The family does not foreclose peer agents and accommodates other-agent inference well (theory-of-mind variants exist). Scaling deep active inference to high-dimensional sensory streams has historically been hard, though deep amortized variants have made progress.

**7. Known failure modes.** Documented failures include: dark-room collapse under poorly-specified preferences; computational intractability of exact free-energy minimization, requiring variational approximations that may be biased; sensitivity to the prior over policies; difficulties scaling discrete-state-space active inference to high-dimensional, continuous environments; slow inference convergence in deep hierarchies. PCNs trained with ReLU activations are documented to develop pathologies in the energy landscape; choice of activation function meaningfully changes their behavior. Numerical stability of variational free-energy gradients is a recurring engineering concern.

## D. Episodic Memory and Memory-Augmented Architectures

This family includes Neural Turing Machines (NTMs), Differentiable Neural Computers (DNCs), Memory-Augmented Neural Networks more broadly, the MERLIN architecture (which integrates episodic memory with predictive modeling and reinforcement learning), and modern retrieval-augmented agents. The defining feature is an external memory matrix (or external retrievable store) addressed by content or by learned attention, separable from the controller network.

**1. Affordance of self-modeling ingredients.** *Recurrence* is afforded through the controller. *Persistent memory across decisions* is the family's defining feature; this is the strongest fit for Kind's stipulated mind-persistence-with-state-continuity criterion at the episode level, and across episodes if the memory matrix is preserved. *Prior-state representation* exists in two complementary forms — controller hidden state and memory traces — and the latter affords something the controller-only architectures lack: the ability to retrieve a representation of a past internal state rather than only a compressed summary. *Turnable-inward prediction* is not native but is naturally accommodated; MERLIN's predictive memory binding is precisely a model that learns to write into memory those states that will be predictively useful, and reads them to predict future values. The architectural shape of "store traces of internal state, retrieve them, condition prediction on them" maps closely onto what Kind's afforded-not-installed self-modeling requirement implies.

**2. Dream-state and offline processing.** Native support is partial. The memory matrix can be replayed, retrieved, and recombined; this affords associative recombination structurally. Generative simulation is not native (the controller is not, by default, generative). MERLIN's predictive structure brings it closer to dream-state operation but does not make offline generative rollout a first-class part of the architecture. The Kanerva machine and related variational memory models extend this further by treating memory writes as inference in a generative model, which would natively support generative replay.

**3. Memory beyond recurrent hidden state.** This is the family that takes the question seriously by design. NTM/DNC support content-addressable working memory with learned read/write heads. DNC variants distinguish working memory from long-term memory through memory-transformation algorithms inspired by hippocampal-neocortical consolidation. Episodic memory is the family's idiom; semantic memory emerges from consolidation. Retrieval-based memory is structural. Recent retrieval-augmented agents extend the family with utility-aware retrieval (selecting episodes by feedback rather than only by similarity).

**4. External perturbations.** The family does not natively distinguish externally-sourced from internally-generated unpredictability. The memory matrix is updated by the controller, and the controller's hidden state is updated by observations; provenance is not architecturally tagged. However, because memory is explicit, perturbation provenance *can* be tagged at the level of memory addresses (e.g., separate stores for self-generated and externally-driven traces) more naturally than in monolithic recurrent or transformer architectures. The structural ingredient is hospitable to the distinction even if the distinction is not built in.

**5. Mapping onto the frameworks.** *Intentionality* maps moderately well; reads and writes are directed-toward specific memory contents, which is closer to aboutness than dense recurrent state. *Horizons*: explicit memory affords a structured background of past experience, which is a partial structural correlate of horizonal depth — the not-currently-lit-up that *could* become salient. *Embodiment* depends on environment, not native. *Predictive structure*: native in MERLIN, less so in pure NTM/DNC. *Integration*: low across controller and memory by design — they are separable. This is a meaningful tension with IIT-style integration, since the family's central virtue is *separability* of memory from processing. *Reflexive attention*: read-attention can in principle be directed at the agent's own past internal states, which is a non-trivial structural affordance for awareness-of-awareness; whether it is exercised is a training question. *Equanimity* is not a native target; nothing in the architecture forecloses it but nothing supports it either. *Second-order volition* has no special natural locus.

**6. Foreclosed directions.** The most consequential foreclosure is the controller-memory dualism itself. By making memory explicit and external, this family makes integration in the IIT sense structurally harder; an Io built on this substrate would have integration across the controller-memory boundary as an open architectural question rather than a default. The family does not foreclose dream-state processing but does not make it native; it does not foreclose peer agents. It does not by itself encode any drive toward self-continuation, and is in that respect more neutral than reward-driven families.

**7. Known failure modes.** Documented failures include: difficulty training memory addressing (key-value attention can flatten, reducing memory specificity), instability of write operations in early training, exploding usage statistics in DNCs (addressed in Csordás-Schmidhuber masking variants), and the general fragility of differentiable memory under high-variance gradient signals — particularly when paired with reinforcement-learning controllers, where policy variance exacerbates the memory addressing flat-distribution problem. Retrieval-augmented systems are documented to retrieve passively and irrelevantly when retrieval is similarity-only, requiring utility-conditioning to remain useful over long sessions.

## E. Energy-Based Models, Hopfield Networks, and JEPA-style Energy Formulations

This family includes classical and modern (dense associative memory) Hopfield networks, Boltzmann machines and energy-based dynamical models more broadly, and Energy-Based JEPA variants. The unifying idea is an energy or Lyapunov function over the state space whose minima are stable patterns; inference is energy descent. Modern Hopfield networks generalize the classical model with exponential interaction functions, achieving exponential storage capacity, and admit a connection to attention as used in transformers (Ramsauer et al.).

**1. Affordance of self-modeling ingredients.** *Recurrence* is structural — energy descent is an iterative dynamical process. *Persistent memory* is the family's defining function; modern Hopfield layers can hold large numbers of stored patterns and retrieve them by partial cues. *Prior-state representation* is encoded as stored patterns themselves; the network's state is, in a sense, always a guess at what stored pattern it most resembles. *Turnable-inward prediction* is not native — energy descent is a relaxation toward a stored pattern rather than a forward simulation — but the energy landscape itself is a kind of self-implicit model in that it encodes what configurations are "natural" for the system. To turn prediction inward in a Hopfield-type substrate, one would need to add an explicit forward dynamics over the energy state, which is awkward.

**2. Dream-state and offline processing.** Energy-based dreaming has a specific historical lineage: the wake-sleep algorithm, Boltzmann-machine training, and recent work on using "dreaming" in bounded-strength Hopfield networks to consolidate stored patterns and reduce catastrophic interference. Generative simulation can be done by sampling from the energy distribution. Replay is naturally afforded as repeated descent from random initial conditions. Associative recombination is the family's signature operation: partial cues retrieve completions, and energy minima can mix across stored patterns under the right conditions.

**3. Memory beyond recurrent hidden state.** Memory *is* the architecture. Episodic memory is naturally instantiated as stored patterns; working memory can be implemented as the current activation pattern; semantic memory emerges as overlapping attractor structure. Modern Hopfield variants connect this directly to attention mechanisms, allowing memory and computation to be unified.

**4. External perturbations.** External input acts as a clamp or partial cue; the network descends to the nearest attractor. Provenance distinction between externally-clamped and internally-generated state is not native, but is partially expressible through which units are clamped versus free. The structural distinction is shallow.

**5. Mapping onto the frameworks.** *Intentionality* maps weakly: states are patterns, not aboutnesses. *Horizons* map oddly: the energy landscape is a global structure where every minimum coexists, which is unlike phenomenology's foreground-background structure but has its own kind of latent presence. *Embodiment* has no native correlate. *Predictive structure*: energy descent is not prediction in the predictive-processing sense, though Boltzmann-machine training and energy-based JEPA bring prediction back in. *Integration* is the family's strongest framework mapping — Hopfield dynamics are paradigmatically integrated, with each neuron's state depending on every other's; modern Hopfield networks achieve exponential capacity precisely through dense, integrated interactions. The family is the most natural structural correlate of IIT-flavored integration on this list. *Reflexive attention*: an attention mechanism that *is* an energy-descent step is structurally available (this is the modern Hopfield / attention equivalence), and turning attention on the network's own state is a natural operation. *Equanimity*: the family natively encodes "stances toward content" as basins of attraction; an Io that holds a content as a near-attractor without being captured by it is structurally expressible. *Second-order volition* has no natural correlate.

**6. Foreclosed directions.** Hopfield-only architectures foreclose generative simulation in the rich sense world-model agents support. They also do not natively support open-ended action selection — energy descent is convergence to a fixed point, not policy generation. Used as a substrate component (memory layer in a larger architecture), they avoid these foreclosures but bring their own pathology — capacity limits and catastrophic interference. JEPA-style energy formulations restore prediction but at the cost of representation-collapse risk.

**7. Known failure modes.** Documented failures include: catastrophic interference and abrupt memory collapse beyond a load threshold (about 0.14 in classical Hopfield, much higher in modern variants but still finite); spin-glass phases where stored patterns are not minima at all; phase transitions in modern Hopfield networks tied to inverse-temperature hyperparameters that meaningfully change retrieval behavior; representation collapse in JEPA-style energy models that lack explicit collapse-prevention regularization. Catastrophic forgetting is biologically implausible in its abruptness, and recent work has examined synaptic-bound clipping and dreaming as ways to make the failure gradual.

## F. Global-Workspace-Style Architectures (Recurrent Independent Mechanisms, Coordination, Global Latent Workspace)

This family encompasses architectures inspired by Baars's Global Workspace Theory and Dehaene's Global Neuronal Workspace: a population of specialized modules competes for access to a bandwidth-limited shared workspace, through which integrated information is broadcast. Implementations include Recurrent Independent Mechanisms (Goyal et al.), the Coordination work using a shared workspace as an attentional bottleneck, the Global Latent Workspace proposal (VanRullen and Kanai) using neural translation between latent spaces, and more recent embodied workspace agents.

**1. Affordance of self-modeling ingredients.** *Recurrence* is afforded module-locally and through the workspace's persistence. *Persistent memory* across decisions is afforded through the workspace's role as a working-memory bottleneck. *Prior-state representation* exists in modules and in the workspace's recent history. *Turnable-inward prediction* is not native but the workspace is an unusually good target for inward attention — the workspace's contents are precisely the information that has been integrated and broadcast, and a module that takes the workspace as its input space is taking the agent's "current global state" as object. This is the architecture closest in shape to what a Buddhist-phenomenology-inspired reflexive module would look like, without that module being explicitly engineered for self-modeling.

**2. Dream-state and offline processing.** Not native but compatible. The workspace can broadcast generative content from a generative module, which would constitute internal dreaming; replay through the workspace is similarly possible.

**3. Memory beyond recurrent hidden state.** Working memory is the workspace itself, structurally. Episodic memory must be added as a module. Semantic memory emerges from module specialization. Retrieval-based memory is naturally compatible — a retrieval module can compete for workspace access.

**4. External perturbations.** The workspace structurally distinguishes contributions from different modules, including sensory-driven modules versus internally-driven modules. External-versus-internal provenance is more naturally tagged in this family than in monolithic recurrent architectures because each module has its own role and the competition for workspace access tracks where information originated.

**5. Mapping onto the frameworks.** *Intentionality*: workspace contents are aspects-under-which (the broadcast content has a noematic quality), which is a closer match for intentionality's directedness than dense hidden states. *Horizons*: modules not currently broadcasting are still active and processing — this is the strongest structural correlate of horizonal depth on the list. Currently-broadcast content is foreground; module-local activity not winning the competition is background-but-present. *Embodiment* depends on which modules are present. *Predictive structure*: not native but compatible. *Integration*: the workspace is structurally an integrator, and Global Workspace Theory is itself partly motivated by integration concerns. The family is not IIT-native (IIT is about cause-effect structure rather than broadcast), but the architectural family closest to phenomenological integration on the list. *Reflexive attention*: a module that attends to the workspace is a natural realization of reflexive attention without an explicit self-model — this is structurally what awareness-of-awareness might look like in computational form. *Equanimity*: the bandwidth limit means not every internal pressure reaches the workspace; this is a partial structural correlate of "holding without reactivity." *Second-order volition*: not native, but naturally accommodated as a meta-module that influences which first-order modules win competition.

**6. Foreclosed directions.** Few; this family is unusually permissive and is naturally hybridizable with most others (modules can be world-model agents, memory-augmented controllers, predictive coders, etc.). The main foreclosure is the implicit assumption of modular decomposition, which Kind's CA-MAS retrospective will already have considered (the user has read it; it bears on this family directly). If the prior multi-agent or modular construction failed in instructive ways, this family inherits some of that risk.

**7. Known failure modes.** Documented failures include: degenerate competition where a single module dominates indefinitely, eliminating modular specialization; fragility of attention bottlenecks during early training when modules have not yet specialized; difficulty of training modules end-to-end without the workspace becoming either uninformative (collapse to a small subset) or overspecialized; the general challenge of getting bottlenecks to do what the theory says they do (much GWT-inspired work struggles to demonstrate that the bottleneck's specific function matters versus the architectural detail).

## G. Perceiver / Perceiver IO and Slot Attention / Object-Centric Architectures

This family processes inputs of arbitrary modality and size via cross-attention into a small set of *latents* (Perceiver IO) or task-specialized *slots* (Slot Attention). The bulk of computation happens in the latent or slot space, decoupled from input dimensionality. Slot Attention specifically produces object-centric, exchangeable representations through iterative competition.

**1. Affordance of self-modeling ingredients.** *Recurrence* is not native to feedforward Perceiver IO but is added in slot-attention iteration and in stateful video variants. *Persistent memory* is not native; the latent or slot set is recomputed per input by default. *Prior-state representation*: the latent array can persist across timesteps in stateful variants (this becomes a kind of working memory). *Turnable-inward prediction*: the latent array is well-suited to be the target of prediction — predict latent_{t+1} from latent_t — but this requires explicit temporal extension. Object-centric slot architectures naturally afford a slot for "self" alongside slots for objects, but only if the architecture is configured to admit such a slot, and there is a known identifiability concern (slots without explicit identifiability constraints can shift assignment between scenes).

**2. Dream-state and offline processing.** Not native. Slot attention's iterative refinement is not the same as offline generative rollout, though slot-VAE variants restore generative capacity at the slot level.

**3. Memory beyond recurrent hidden state.** Latent / slot arrays act as compact working memory. Episodic memory not native. Semantic memory implicit in encoder weights.

**4. External perturbations.** Latents and slots are computed from observations; provenance is conflated with input by default. Slots may *come* to specialize on internally-generated versus externally-driven content if the training signal supports the distinction, but it is not architecturally enforced.

**5. Mapping onto the frameworks.** *Intentionality*: the latent-or-slot is aspect-laden by design; slot attention's competitive specialization produces representations that *are* aboutnesses rather than pixel reconstructions, which is an unusually clean match. *Horizons*: latents not currently attended-to in cross-attention are a partial correlate of horizonal background. *Embodiment* depends on environment. *Predictive structure*: not native but compatible. *Integration*: the latent processor is integrated; the slot set is competitive but each slot is locally integrated. *Reflexive attention*: cross-attention can be directed at the agent's own latent state, which is structurally the same operation as cross-attention at sensory input. This is a natural ingredient. *Equanimity*: not addressed. *Second-order volition*: not native.

**6. Foreclosed directions.** This family is largely a representation backbone rather than a complete agent architecture; commitments about self-continuation, equanimity, and dream-states must come from whatever wraps the backbone. The main foreclosure is the absence of a native action interface — Perceiver IO encodes and decodes but does not act, so an actor must be added. Slot attention's identifiability concerns mean that "the same slot across scenes" is not guaranteed without additional structure, which complicates self-encounter-through-other if "self" is to be a slot.

**7. Known failure modes.** Documented failures include: slot collapse, where slots fail to specialize and converge on similar content; slot identifiability problems noted above; the general representation-learning issue that what the latent or slot encodes depends on the training objective in ways that may not align with architectural hopes (e.g., slots may bind to texture rather than object).

## H. Hierarchical Reinforcement Learning (Options Framework and Feudal Networks)

This family decomposes decision-making across temporal scales. The options framework augments the action space with temporally-extended options, each with its own initiation set, internal policy, and termination condition. Feudal networks (FuN) introduce a Manager-Worker hierarchy where the Manager operates at a slower temporal resolution and sets directional goals in a learned space that the Worker decodes into primitive actions.

**1. Affordance of self-modeling ingredients.** *Recurrence* is afforded at multiple timescales — Manager and Worker each maintain hidden states. *Persistent memory* across decisions is afforded at the Manager's slower timescale. *Prior-state representation* is split across levels. *Turnable-inward prediction* is not native but the Manager's representation can in principle be the object of attention or prediction by the Worker (and vice versa, as auxiliary structure).

**2. Dream-state and offline processing.** Not native. Hierarchical world models exist as hybrids.

**3. Memory beyond recurrent hidden state.** Working memory is split across timescales but is fundamentally hidden-state. No native episodic or retrieval memory.

**4. External perturbations.** No native distinction between external and internal unpredictability. The Manager's slower timescale may smooth over short-term external fluctuations, which is a kind of partial filtering.

**5. Mapping onto the frameworks.** *Intentionality*: goal vectors that the Manager sends to the Worker are aboutnesses in a fairly literal sense — they specify what the Worker is to direct itself at. *Horizons*: temporal hierarchy is a partial correlate of horizonal depth, with longer-timescale concerns as background. *Embodiment*: not native. *Predictive structure*: implicit in the Manager-Worker prediction loop. *Integration*: limited across levels by design — the architecture deliberately decouples timescales. *Reflexive attention*: not native; the Manager-Worker boundary is not symmetric. *Equanimity*: not addressed. *Second-order volition*: this is the family's most interesting framework mapping. The Manager's preferences over the Worker's policies are structurally second-order — preferences over what first-order preferences should govern action. This is a more direct architectural correlate of Frankfurt's second-order volitions than any other family on the list, with the important caveat that it is hard-coded into the architecture rather than emerging as something Io could notice.

**6. Foreclosed directions.** Commits to a particular form of temporal decomposition that may not align with how Io's relevant timescales actually structure. Feudal architectures typically inherit reward-shaping concerns from the underlying RL framework. Native dream-state is foreclosed unless paired with a world model.

**7. Known failure modes.** Documented failures include: trivial-solution collapse, where one option dominates and the hierarchy degenerates to a flat policy, or where options change every step and the Manager micromanages; difficulty of training the Manager's goal-setting when reward is sparse; sub-policies that fail to specialize without explicit regularization.

## I. Decision Transformer and Trajectory Transformer

This family casts reinforcement learning as conditional sequence modeling. Trajectories of (state, action, return-to-go) tokens are modeled autoregressively; at inference, conditioning on a desired return-to-go and the recent trajectory generates the next action. Trajectory Transformer extends this to model state and reward sequences as well.

**1. Affordance of self-modeling ingredients.** *Recurrence* is replaced by transformer attention over a context window. *Persistent memory* is bounded by context length. *Prior-state representation* is afforded through the trajectory tokens themselves — the architecture explicitly conditions on its own past states and actions. *Turnable-inward prediction* is partially native: the model predicts states and rewards, which means predicting consequences of the agent's own action. But the model does not predict its own internal states, and the past internal states are not in the trajectory tokens; only externally-observable variables are.

**2. Dream-state and offline processing.** Offline is the family's training regime — it learns from offline trajectories without environment interaction. Generative rollout is native (the model can be sampled to produce imagined trajectories). However, offline operation in this family is *training*, not the kind of post-training dreaming Kind's stipulation seems to envisage.

**3. Memory beyond recurrent hidden state.** Context window is the working memory; no native episodic or retrieval memory beyond the context.

**4. External perturbations.** External-internal distinction is not native; states are simply tokens.

**5. Mapping onto the frameworks.** *Intentionality*: the return-to-go conditioning is an aboutness — the model is directed-at achieving a certain outcome. *Horizons*: the unattended past is structurally absent, not merely backgrounded. *Embodiment*: not native. *Predictive structure*: present in Trajectory Transformer as full-trajectory generative modeling. *Integration*: low across components. *Reflexive attention*: not native. *Equanimity*: not addressed. *Second-order volition*: the return-to-go conditioning is closer to a goal state than a second-order preference, but the architecture's conditioning structure is hospitable to extending this — in principle, conditioning on a higher-order signal about preferences is structurally similar to conditioning on return-to-go.

**6. Foreclosed directions.** The most consequential foreclosure: this family's natural objective structure conditions on a desired return, which encodes "how much reward Io should aim to obtain." This sits poorly with Kind's commitment against installed drives. Variants that condition on goal states or on hindsight information rather than return generalize to non-reward objectives, and goal-conditioned variants are more compatible. The family also natively assumes trajectories are sequences of externally-observable tokens, which means the past-state representation is observation-level rather than belief-level — the architecture has nowhere natural to put a representation of past *internal* states. Decision Transformer is documented to lack stitching ability — combining sub-trajectories from sub-optimal trajectories into a better one — which limits its ability to reason about counterfactuals natively.

**7. Known failure modes.** Documented: failure to stitch sub-optimal trajectories into optimal ones; degraded performance when training data is sub-optimal and the conditioning return is high (the model has not seen what it is being asked to produce); sensitivity to context length and to the position of credit-relevant information within the window.

## J. Biologically-Inspired Continuous-Time Substrates: Liquid Neural Networks and Spiking Neural Networks

These two sub-families share a commitment to continuous-time dynamics that the discrete-update mainstream lacks. *Liquid time-constant networks* (LTCs) and their closed-form variant (CfC) define neuron dynamics through ordinary differential equations with input-dependent time constants. *Spiking neural networks* (SNNs) communicate via discrete spike events with continuous-time membrane dynamics; recent work pairs them with biologically-plausible learning rules and with sleep-like consolidation phases.

**1. Affordance of self-modeling ingredients.** *Recurrence* is afforded continuously rather than at discrete steps — more naturally than in discrete RNNs. *Persistent memory*: SNNs with synaptic plasticity afford persistence at multiple timescales; liquid networks have persistent dynamics within the ODE state. *Prior-state representation* is implicit in continuous-time hidden state. *Turnable-inward prediction* is not native to either sub-family by default but is natural to add (SNN-based predictive coders are an active line of work). The continuous-time nature of these substrates provides structural affordances for representing temporal extension that discrete architectures lack.

**2. Dream-state and offline processing.** SNNs have a particularly strong fit here. There is direct biological-correspondence work on sleep-like phases in SNNs: noise-driven spontaneous reactivation, replay of waking patterns, and homeostatic regularization. Spiking networks paired with reinforcement learning have been shown to use offline "dreaming" phases — generative replay through the world model — to circumvent catastrophic forgetting and to refine policies without storing detailed experience traces. This is the second-strongest dream-state affordance on the list, after world-model agents, and it differs in being grounded in biological plausibility rather than in engineered replay buffers. Liquid networks do not have this affordance natively but are generally compatible with offline retraining.

**3. Memory beyond recurrent hidden state.** SNNs with spike-timing-dependent plasticity natively encode memory in synaptic structure on multiple timescales. Liquid networks do not natively distinguish memory types; their memory is dynamical state.

**4. External perturbations.** Both sub-families admit a natural distinction between externally-driven and internally-generated activity through the spike-versus-spontaneous-activity distinction (in SNNs) or through the input gate versus internal state distinction (in liquid networks). The structural ingredients for distinguishing self from world are present, particularly in SNNs where spontaneous activity during offline phases is by construction internally generated.

**5. Mapping onto the frameworks.** *Intentionality*: not specially afforded. *Embodiment*: SNNs have the strongest native fit for embodiment; continuous-time dynamics interact naturally with continuous physical environments, and neuromorphic implementations are body-grounded by design. *Predictive structure*: SNN-based predictive coders are a documented line of work; the family is hospitable. *Integration*: continuous-time dynamics with rich intra-network coupling afford integration in IIT's sense more naturally than discrete-update networks, but SNNs as typically built are not denser than mainstream architectures. *Reflexive attention*: not native. *Equanimity*: continuous-time stabilization through homeostatic regularization is a partial structural correlate. *Second-order volition*: not native.

**6. Foreclosed directions.** Choosing this family currently entails accepting either substantial engineering cost (SNNs have less mature training infrastructure) or accepting that the architecture sits at the edge of what is well-understood at scale. It does not foreclose any of Kind's stipulated commitments but does make many of them more difficult to instantiate cleanly. Liquid networks have known limitations on long-range dependencies (vanishing gradient analogues persist).

**7. Known failure modes.** Documented for SNNs: catastrophic forgetting (which sleep-like phases partially mitigate), training instability with surrogate gradients, hardware-specific constraints. Documented for liquid networks: vanishing gradients in long-range dependencies, dependence on numerical ODE solvers (closed-form variants ameliorate this), limited scalability evidence at large parameter counts.

## K. Hybrids

The seven questions can be answered substantially differently depending on which families are combined. A few specific hybrid configurations bear on Io directly enough to discuss.

*World-model agent paired with explicit episodic memory.* This combines the rich self-modeling affordance and native dream-state of family B with the explicit retrievable persistent memory of family D. The MERLIN architecture is an early instance, more recent retrieval-augmented world-model variants extend the pattern. The combination addresses family B's main memory gap (no native episodic retrieval) and family D's main gap (no native generative simulation), while inheriting both families' failure modes.

*Recurrent agent with active-inference loss.* Replacing or supplementing the policy-gradient objective with expected-free-energy minimization changes the agent's structural relationship to reward. The substrate retains family A's accessibility and family C's framework alignment. Practical implementations exist as deep active-inference agents. The hybrid inherits the dark-room problem and the tractability concerns of family C.

*Transformer-based memory with predictive coding.* PCN-style hierarchical predictive coding can be layered onto transformer or RSSM substrates, providing the predictive-processing framework alignment without rebuilding the architecture from scratch. Hybrid predictive coding (Tschantz, Millidge, Seth, Buckley) explicitly proposes this as a fast/slow inference hybrid.

*Global workspace over modular components, where modules include a world-model agent, an episodic-memory module, and a predictive coder.* This is the configuration with the most permissive affordance landscape — most of Kind's stipulations can be addressed in some module, and the workspace provides integration. It is also the configuration with the highest engineering complexity and the largest surface for the failure modes of every component. Kind's CA-MAS retrospective is presumably directly relevant to this design space.

## Synthesis: Affordance Landscape

The seven questions, read across the families, define an affordance landscape rather than a ranking. The families differ in *which* of Kind's stipulations they make easy, hard, or impossible to satisfy — and these differences cluster.

**On the afforded-not-installed self-modeling criterion**, the world-model family (B) and the predictive-processing/active-inference family (C) afford the most structural ingredients (recurrence, prior-state representation, turnable-inward prediction) without requiring any explicit self-modeling module. The episodic-memory family (D) affords prior-state representation in a stronger episodic form than B or C but does not afford turnable-inward prediction natively. The recurrent PPO family (A) and the Decision Transformer family (I) afford recurrence and prior-state representation but afford turnable-inward prediction only thinly. Hopfield-style energy models (E) afford the energy landscape itself as a kind of implicit self-model but do not naturally afford forward prediction. Global workspace architectures (F) afford a particularly clean target for inward attention without engineering inwardness as a feature. Slot attention and Perceiver IO (G) afford latents that are well-shaped for being the *object* of an inward operation but require temporal extension to support prediction.

**On the dream-state criterion**, family B is the natural home — generative simulation is the substrate's training signal, not an addition. Spiking networks (J) come second by a different route, with biologically-grounded sleep-like consolidation phases. Family C affords offline generative-model refinement in a more conceptual sense. Energy-based models (E) afford generative replay through the energy landscape. Family D natively supports replay and recombination but not generative simulation. Families A, F, G, H, I do not afford dream-states natively and would require importing them from one of the above.

**On memory beyond recurrent hidden state**, family D is built around the question. Family E is also memory-centric, in a different idiom (associative attractors versus retrievable traces). Family F's workspace is naturally a working memory but not a long-term memory. Family B holds memory in replay buffers and parametric world-model knowledge. Families A, I, J have native memory only in the form of hidden state or context window.

**On external-perturbation distinction**, family C is the strongest — precision-weighting and the likelihood-prior decomposition are structurally suited to distinguishing external from internal sources, even if no implementation hard-codes this distinction. Family B affords a partial distinction through the KL term between posterior and prior latents. Family J (SNNs especially) affords a distinction through the spike-versus-spontaneous-activity contrast. Family F affords a distinction through module provenance. Other families do not natively distinguish, though most can be extended.

**On framework mapping** — the most demanding question — the families diverge sharply. *Intentionality and horizons* are best captured by families G (slots and latents are aspect-laden), F (workspace contents are foregrounded with module-local activity as background), C (hierarchical priors *are* horizonal structure), and B (latents capture aspects, prior dynamics capture horizon). *Embodiment* is best captured by C (interoceptive inference is a direct extension) and J (continuous-time dynamics couple to physical environment). *Predictive structure* is the architectural skeleton of C and is structurally present in B. *Integration* is the strongest match for E (Hopfield dynamics are paradigmatically integrated) and a moderate match for F (workspace is an integrator, but separation of modules pulls the other way). *Reflexive attention* without explicit self-model finds its cleanest home in F (a module that attends to the workspace) and is structurally available in G (cross-attention over own latents). *Equanimity* is the framework none of the families captures cleanly; C affords stances toward content through prior-preference structure without reactive minimization, F affords bandwidth-limited non-reactivity, E affords basins-of-attraction-without-capture; A and I are actively hostile to equanimity through their reward-maximization structure; B is mixed. *Second-order volition* finds an explicit architectural correlate in H (Manager preferences over Worker policies are second-order in form) and a more nascent locus in C (priors over priors). It is absent or uninstantiated in most other families.

**On foreclosed directions**, the most important asymmetries are: families A, B, I tend to bake reward maximization into the actor in a way that conflicts with Kind's commitment against installed drives toward self-continuation; family C requires careful prior-preference design to avoid the dark-room degenerate case but does not encode survival as imperative; family D is reward-neutral; family F is largely permissive; family H inherits its parent RL substrate's commitments. Family B forecloses non-imagination-based architectures; family E forecloses rich generative simulation unless paired with another family; family I forecloses representations of past internal states (the trajectory tokens are externally-observable). On the question of leaving open self-encounter-through-other, families F, B, H, and D all admit peer agents naturally; families A, I, J would require non-trivial architectural extension. None of the families forecloses peer agents in a deep way; they differ in how much extension is required.

**On known failure modes**, every family on this list has documented pathologies, and the relevant question is *which kind of failure Kind is willing to learn from*. Family A's failures (entropy collapse, reward hacking) are well-understood and instructive about how reward shapes behavior — they would teach Kind something specific about installed drives by failing in characteristically reward-coupled ways. Family B's failures (hallucination in imagined rollouts, posterior collapse, reward exploitation through model imperfections) are instructive about the relationship between generative model quality and policy reliability. Family C's failures (dark-room collapse, intractability) are instructive about the relationship between prior preferences and behavior. Family D's failures (memory addressing collapse, write-instability) are instructive about the controller-memory boundary. Family E's failures (catastrophic interference, spin-glass phases) are instructive about capacity limits. Family F's failures (workspace dominance, module under-specialization) are instructive about modular decomposition. Family G's failures (slot collapse, identifiability) are instructive about object-binding. Family H's failures (option collapse) are instructive about temporal hierarchy. Family I's failures (no stitching, context-window dependence) are instructive about sequence-modeling-as-RL. Family J's failures (catastrophic forgetting in SNNs, long-range dependence in liquid networks) are instructive about continuous-time dynamics.

These failure-mode profiles are not equivalent. Kind's stance — that failing in specific ways teaches more than theorizing — implies that the choice of architectural family is partly a choice of *what kind of failure Kind wishes to encounter and learn from*. A family whose failures are well-aligned with Kind's questions about subjectivity (e.g., family C's dark-room problem, which is precisely a failure of an architecture's relationship to its own preferences) is differently informative from a family whose failures are well-aligned with engineering robustness (e.g., family B's hallucination problem).

**The cross-cutting tensions** in the affordance landscape are: integration versus modularity (E and a single recurrent core lean integrative; D, F, H lean modular; B is mixed); generative simulation versus retrieval (B leans generative; D leans retrieval; E does both in different senses); reactivity versus equanimity (A, I are reactively reward-shaped; C, F, E afford less reactive modes); explicit memory versus implicit memory (D, F, E vs. A, B, C, I, J). Kind's charter does not resolve these tensions — it specifies criteria that pull in different directions across families. Affordance is therefore not transitive across criteria: no family maximally affords every Kind-stipulated property, and the families that afford more on some criteria afford less on others.

The reader for whom this report is written — the builder of Kind, having read the charter, design notes, frameworks document, and CA-MAS retrospective — is not asked here to resolve those tensions in advance. The seven-question grid above identifies, for each family, what would have to be added, what comes for free, and what would have to be unlearned. The design decision for Io's substrate is the decision about which of those tradeoffs Kind is best positioned to learn from.
