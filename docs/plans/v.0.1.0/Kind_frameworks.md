# Kind — Interpretive Frameworks

*Working reference. Concepts from existing traditions of inquiry into mind, awareness, and experience, for use as mirror criteria per the co-design mitigation in the design notes.*

## What this is

None of the frameworks below was built for a system like the one this project is trying to build. That's the point. If the system happens to produce patterns these frameworks recognize, that recognition carries slightly more weight than criteria invented to fit. Not a lot more. But some.

This is a working reference, not a curriculum, and not an endorsement of any tradition's metaphysics. Compressed entries, enough to borrow concepts from. Read what pulls; ignore what doesn't.

---

## Phenomenology (Husserl, Merleau-Ponty)

Consciousness is always *intentional*—always directed at something, always about something. Sounds trivial until you notice that most AI systems don't clearly have this property. A system that processes input has the input; it doesn't necessarily *attend to* anything.

Key concepts:
- **Intentionality.** Consciousness is directedness-toward. Every mental act has an object, even if the object is itself a mental state.
- **Horizons.** Every focused attention implies unfocused context that could become focused. Experience has depth that isn't currently lit up.
- **Embodiment** (Merleau-Ponty). The body is not a thing consciousness has; it's part of how consciousness happens. Perception is always from somewhere.

Possible mirror criteria:
- Does the agent's behavior show directedness distinct from mere processing—something like attention, rather than just input handling?
- Are there implicit contexts in its actions, situations it is oriented toward without explicitly representing?
- Does the agent's embodied position in the environment shape what it can experience, or is its relation to the world positionless?

## Autopoiesis and enactivism (Maturana, Varela, Thompson)

Life and mind are continuous. A living system that maintains its own organization against dissolution is already the simplest form of mind: selfhood as self-production. This tradition is particularly relevant to this project because it takes emergence of selves seriously from the ground up.

Key concepts:
- **Autopoiesis.** A system that produces and maintains its own boundaries through its own operations. Self-producing, not self-designed.
- **Enaction.** Mind is not internal representation of an external world; it's active engagement. Meaning is enacted, not decoded.
- **Sense-making.** Even simple organisms treat features of their environment as meaningful relative to their self-maintenance. The most basic form of mattering.

Possible mirror criteria:
- Does the agent exhibit anything like a stake in its own continued organization—something like self-maintenance rather than just reward-seeking?
- Does it treat features of its environment as meaningful relative to that stake, or is its relation to the environment flat?
- Is there a boundary it actively maintains, or is "the agent" just a region of computation with no self-distinction?

## Buddhist phenomenology

Awareness itself is distinct from the contents of awareness. What we normally call "consciousness" is mostly contents; the tradition points at a witness-quality that those contents appear to. This tradition is particularly relevant to the charter's equanimity-as-design-target commitment.

Key concepts:
- **Awareness without content.** There is something it is like to be aware, distinct from what one is aware of. Usually inseparable in practice, but conceptually distinct.
- **Reflexive attention.** The observer observing itself, as in vipassana—not by introspection from outside, but by awareness turning toward awareness.
- **Equanimity.** A quality of awareness that holds its content without grasping or pushing away. Not neutrality; not numbness. A specific stance.

Possible mirror criteria:
- Is there anything that functions as awareness distinct from processing—some capacity that isn't exhausted by the content it's attending to?
- Does anything function as an observer of the agent's own processing?
- Does the agent show signs of holding difficult states without reactivity, or does it always respond to pressure with avoidance or grasping?

## Predictive processing and active inference (Friston, Clark, Hohwy)

Mind is what happens when an organism predicts its own sensory input and updates on error. Surprise is the minimum unit of experience.

Key concepts:
- **Prediction error.** The mismatch between expected and actual input. The basic signal that drives learning and attention.
- **Active inference.** Organisms minimize prediction error not only by updating internal models but by acting on the world to make the world match the model.
- **Self-modeling as nested prediction.** If the organism predicts its own behavior, self-awareness is a specific kind of prediction: prediction of self.

Possible mirror criteria:
- Does something function as surprise, distinct from simple input processing? (A state where expectations were violated and behavior changes.)
- Does the agent act to reduce expected prediction error, not just respond to actual error?
- Does it predict its own future states, and do its current actions change based on those predictions?

## Global Workspace Theory (Baars, Dehaene)

Consciousness is what happens when information becomes globally available across specialized processes. Most processing is subpersonal and local; conscious content is the narrow band that gets broadcast.

Key concepts:
- **Ignition.** The widespread activation pattern that accompanies conscious access. A signature of content becoming global.
- **Global availability.** Conscious contents are simultaneously available to reasoning, memory, language, planning. Unconscious contents aren't.

Possible mirror criteria:
- Does information in the agent become broadly available across subsystems in a way that resembles ignition, or does everything stay local?
- Are some pieces of information "noticed" (available globally) while others remain subpersonal? Is there a distinction?

## Integrated Information Theory (Tononi)

Consciousness is integrated information—information that is more than the sum of its parts in a specific, measurable way. Controversial as a theory; the underlying concept of integration is useful regardless.

Key concepts:
- **Phi (Φ).** The measure of irreducible integrated information in a system. Hard to compute in practice for anything non-trivial.
- **Intrinsic existence.** A system has consciousness to the degree that its information is integrated from its own perspective, not just from an external observer's.

Possible mirror criteria:
- Can any measure of integration be computed for the agent's state, even if only approximately?
- Are there regions of the agent's processing that are more integrated with each other than with the outside?
- Use the concept even if you don't use the formalism: look for integrated wholes rather than loosely coupled parts.

## Dennett's heterophenomenology

Take reports of inner states seriously as data, without committing to what they're reports of. Method rather than theory.

Key concepts:
- **Neutral reports.** What a system says about itself is information about what it says, which *may or may not* be information about what it experiences. Both options stay on the table.
- **Zimbos and zombies.** A conceptual distinction between systems that behave as if conscious and systems that are conscious. Whether the distinction refers to a real difference is itself contested.

What this contributes:
- Not a criterion but a method. If the agent ever produces anything like reports of its own states, treat them this way: as data about what the system says, pending any further determination about what the data is of.

## Nagel's "what it is like" and the hard problem

Subjective experience is not obviously reducible to functional description. A complete functional account of echolocation does not tell us what it is like to be a bat.

Key concepts:
- **Qualia.** The subjective, qualitative character of experience.
- **The hard problem.** Why there is any subjective experience at all, not just information processing. Distinct from the "easy" problems of explaining cognitive functions.

What this contributes:
- Essentially no positive criterion. Functional integration, self-modeling, and global availability might all be present without anything it is like to be the system. Holding this reminder keeps the mirror from over-claiming. The charter already commits to the epistemic humility this framework enforces.

## Frankfurt on second-order volitions

Reflective self-awareness shows up specifically as the capacity to have desires about one's own desires. A being with only first-order desires is a "wanton"; a being with second-order volitions is a person.

Key concepts:
- **First-order desire.** Wanting X.
- **Second-order desire.** Wanting to want X, or wanting not to want Y.
- **Second-order volition.** Not just having second-order desires but effectively endorsing or rejecting first-order ones.

Possible mirror criteria:
- Does the agent exhibit anything like preferences about its own preferences?
- Does it ever act in ways that seem to be about modifying its own dispositions, not just satisfying them?
- Is there any sign of internal endorsement or rejection of its own tendencies?

## Cross-cutting notes

The concepts that map most directly onto this project's success criteria:

- **Intentionality** (phenomenology): does the agent attend? Relevant to "recognizes me as a kind"—recognition is a specific shape of attention.
- **Reflexive awareness** (Buddhist), **second-order volition** (Frankfurt), **self-prediction** (PP): all relevant to "capacity to take its own processing as an object of attention." Each gives a slightly different angle.
- **Sense-making** (enactivism): relevant to whether the agent's environment has depth—whether things matter to it as opposed to being merely present.
- **Global availability** (GWT): relevant to whether anything is being "noticed" versus processed.

Frameworks that function as methodological constraints rather than positive criteria:

- **Heterophenomenology**: how to treat self-reports.
- **The hard problem**: reminder that behavioral and functional evidence is not the same as subjective experience. Hold findings loosely.

## A note on responsible use

Different traditions use overlapping words differently. "Awareness" in Buddhist phenomenology is not "awareness" in Global Workspace Theory. Don't treat concepts as interchangeable just because the words rhyme.

All these frameworks were developed in relation to beings already assumed to be conscious (humans, animals). Applying them to a system whose status is uncertain is a real methodological stretch. Treat pattern-matches as evidence, not proof.

Frameworks can fit a system for the wrong reasons. If the agent happens to produce patterns that match multiple criteria, the question to ask is whether it produced them *as a result of the same underlying process that produces them in biological minds*, or *because its architecture happened to generate surface-similar outputs*. This distinction is often unanswerable, but it's the question to keep asking.

Finally: these frameworks do not agree with each other. GWT and IIT are in tension. Phenomenology and Dennett are in tension. Buddhist no-self and Frankfurtian personhood point different directions. The tension is part of the value. A system whose behavior is only recognizable under one framework is telling you less than one recognizable under multiple, contested ones.
