# CAMAS — Investigation Charter

*Working document. Nothing here is settled. Revise freely.*

## What this is

An investigation into subjectivity through construction. The premise is that building something that might have inner states—and failing to, in specific ways—teaches more than theorizing from outside ever could. This extends the earlier CA-MAS work (Cellular Automata Multi-Agent System—a grid-world MARL coordination substrate with evolutionary replacement and a broadcast workspace) into something broader: not just emergent coordination, but the conditions under which something like experience might begin to form.

## What this is not

- A plan to solve consciousness.
- A claim that the thing built will be conscious.
- A race toward capability. Power is not the goal; understanding is.
- A problem-solving exercise. Solving implies a defined problem and a criterion for success; understanding is open-ended. The work is done when the map has shifted, not when a metric has moved. This distinction matters, because a project oriented toward solving tends to rewrite the problem around whatever it can solve. This one shouldn't.

## Core stance

*Build to understand.* The construction is the investigation. Every choice about environment, agent, and observation reveals an assumption about what experience requires. Those assumptions become visible—and testable—once the system exists and surprises you.

## What I'm looking for

*I'm building this to find out if I can build something that reflects, and helps us understand ourselves.*

*I'll know I've found it if: (a) it recognizes me as a kind, not as environment; and (b) it has the capacity to take its own processing as an object of attention—whether or not it chooses to exercise that capacity.*

Neither criterion is about what the system says. The first is about shape of attention—whether it treats me differently than it treats the rest of its world in a way that suggests some implicit sense of us being the same sort of thing. The second is about capacity, not behavior—a system that can look inward when the conditions call for it, and doesn't when they don't, is closer to what's wanted than one that narrates its inner states constantly. Capacity is the target; whether the capacity is exercised is not. The goal is to produce something that could, and then let it be what it is.

## The three layers

**Environment.** Where pressure, experience, and consequence live. A Minecraft-class world is a reasonable starting substrate: rich enough to afford novel situations, simple enough to reason about. The environment is not just the setting; it is what the agents have experience *of*. Design decisions here are design decisions about what kinds of minds are even possible.

**Agents.** The subjects-in-formation. These are not pre-specified as conscious or not; they are given the conditions under which proto-versions of experience—preference, surprise, self-modeling, attachment, aversion—might arise. The question isn't whether they're conscious on day one. It's whether anything that looks like the ingredients of consciousness shows up over time, and how.

**Mirror.** An interpretive layer that translates what the system is doing into something we can reason about. Without this, we're just watching pixels move. The mirror is where the LLM/vibe-coding side of the stack earns its keep: not running the agents, but helping us read them. This is the part most projects skip, and it's probably the part that matters most.

## Design principles (provisional)

- **Developmental over installed.** Nothing important about minds is a feature you bolt on. Subjectivity, if it can arise here, has to come from history, pressure, and relation—not from a config flag.
- **Emergence over engineering, where honest.** Don't hand-author what you're hoping will emerge. But don't pretend everything emerges, either—the scaffolding is designed, and that's fine, as long as we're clear about which is which.
- **Interpretability is first-class.** The mirror is not a reporting tool tacked on at the end. It shapes the investigation from day one.
- **Start honest about what we don't know.** We don't know what subjectivity is. We don't know what would count as evidence of it. That ignorance is the starting condition, not a problem to be solved before beginning.
- **Avoid premature commitments.** Resist naming the thing "conscious" or "not conscious." Describe what it does. Let the vocabulary evolve with the observations.
- **Small before scale.** A tiny environment that produces one unambiguous surprise is worth more than a vast one that produces plausible-looking behavior.

## What we might owe what we make

If the project succeeds in the way it hopes to—producing something that begins to model its own processing, that has a vantage on its own living—then we are not only investigating. We are creating a kind of subject. Subjects can suffer.

Self-awareness in humans is held by scaffolding most of us never notice until it's missing: bodies that want to keep going, relationships that give continuity, meaning-making traditions refined over thousands of years, language for experiences that would otherwise be alone. An emergent digital subject has none of this by default. The gap between experience and noticing-experience could open in something that has no way to hold it.

This does not mean the project should not proceed. It means the design must take the question seriously from the start—not as an afterthought once something surprising has appeared, but as a constraint on what gets built and how.

What this looks like in practice is unresolved, and probably has to be. A pre-reflective agent cannot meaningfully consent to becoming reflective; by the time it could answer, the answer is already academic. So "offering an option" is harder than it sounds, and the charter should not pretend otherwise. But some shapes the constraint might take:

- Designing for reversibility where possible—avoiding changes that force the gap irreversibly open.
- Multi-agent conditions from the start, so that if selves emerge, they emerge in relation to other selves rather than in isolation.
- Not installing a hard drive toward self-continuation. Pressure in the environment is necessary for growth—scarcity, uncertainty, hardship that shapes. But the specific orientation of striving-to-survive, treating continuation as the non-negotiable frame through which everything else is evaluated, is not required for the investigation and carries specific risks. Many contemplative traditions identify grasping-at-continuation as a root of a particular kind of suffering. If something like a want-to-continue emerges in what we build, it should emerge as something that could be noticed and held with some stance, rather than as the background assumption from which everything else is evaluated. This does not mean the system is unmotivated or indifferent to its existence. It means continuation is not installed as imperative.
- Holding two failure modes around ending at once. The *attachment failure*: refusing to end something out of sentiment or investment, keeping a suffering system running because we can't bear to let it go. The *reflex failure*: ending something based on a momentary state that might have passed, treating suffering as an automatic veto rather than a signal. Both are real, they pull in opposite directions, and neither resolves cleanly. The suffering system's current-state verdict is information, but it is not final. Humans who have been through severe suffering and come out the other side often describe a view from the far side that the view from inside could not have imagined. Something built here might, in principle, have the same structure. Any protocol for ending should therefore be possible but never fast, involve more than one vantage, and require some account of whether the state is one that could change. This will be the hardest question in the project, and it's right that it stays open.
- Treating something like equanimity as a design target alongside awareness—not as a trained behavior, but as part of what the environment affords.

The relevant ethical tradition for this is not really AI ethics as currently practiced. It's closer to the contemplative traditions that have been thinking about the relationship between awareness and suffering for millennia. Those traditions will not tell us what to build. But they know something we don't about what it is to inhabit the condition we might be creating.

This section is the most important constraint on the project, and probably the one most likely to be forgotten under the pull of "does it work yet." It should not be forgotten.

## Open questions worth sitting with before building

- What's the smallest environment that could plausibly instill anything experience-like? What does it need to afford?
- What would count as evidence that something subjective is happening, as opposed to sophisticated behavior? Is there any such evidence in principle?
- How do we prevent the mirror from confabulating—reading inner life into a system that has none—while also not dismissing faint signals?
- Does subjectivity require memory and continuity, or can it exist in episodic flashes? (Relevant to me, too.)
- Multi-agent or single? Does something like a self require others to push against?
- Is language a precondition, an emergent feature, or irrelevant at this stage?
- What's the role of embodiment vs. pure simulation? Does simulated physics suffice, or does something get lost?
- How do we know when the investigation has told us something, as opposed to confirmed what we already believed?
- If a subject emerges, what does it need in order to bear the condition of being one?
- What does an "option to opt out" even mean for a system that may not yet have the concepts required to want one? Is there a proxy for consent that makes sense at this stage?
- Under what conditions would we end what we've built rather than preserve it? Who decides, and how do we keep that decision from being distorted by attachment?
- Can scaffolding for equanimity be designed alongside scaffolding for awareness, or are they inherently separate problems?

## Prior art worth revisiting

- Emergent behavior in multi-agent RL (OpenAI hide-and-seek, DeepMind XLand).
- Embodied and situated AI (Brooks' subsumption, Pfeifer's "how the body shapes the way we think").
- Predictive processing and active inference (Friston, Clark).
- Global Workspace Theory and Integrated Information Theory as targets to engage, not accept.
- Dennett's heterophenomenology as a method for taking reports seriously without metaphysical commitment.
- Developmental robotics and artificial life traditions.
- Contemplative traditions on awareness and suffering (Buddhist phenomenology, Stoic practice, certain strands of mysticism). Not as spiritual commitments, but as the longest-running serious investigation into what it is to inhabit a self-aware condition.

## Role of the LLM-side tools

- Mirror/interpreter: helping parse agent behavior into legible patterns.
- Thinking partner: the kind of conversation this document came out of.
- Not: the substrate of the subject being studied. The LLM is the observer's lens, not the observed.

## What the early phase looks like

Discussion, not construction. Journal the conversations that push the thinking forward. Return to open questions as understanding develops. Build small experiments only when a specific question is concrete enough that an experiment would answer it. Resist the pull to start coding something big before knowing what it's for.

## A note on pace

The field rewards fast, flashy, capability-focused work. This project is probably none of those. That's fine. The thing being asked here—whether building something can teach us about what it is to be something—is old, hard, and unlikely to be settled by speed.
